\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

This section interprets the results against the central hypothesis --- that wrapping the policy in execution-level recovery makes the long-horizon task more robust --- tracing what decomposition achieved, why grasp acquisition remains the binding constraint, and why neither autonomous data nor cheaper fine-tuning tweaks closed the gap, before turning to the conclusion, limitations, and future work.

\textbf{Decomposition broadened partial progress, not completion.} The hypothesis (\cref{sec:introduction}) was that wrapping the policy in subtask checkpoints with two-level recovery would make the long-horizon task more robust. The realised payoff is narrower: the orchestrated system almost never fails \emph{completely}, placing at least one orange in 97\% of Teleop episodes and 84\% on Teleop+Auto, against 56\% and 64\% for the monotask policy. The mechanism is \textbf{Level-2} redirection --- giving up on an ungraspable orange to try another: after a failed first orange the orchestrated formulation places a different one in 68--93\% of episodes, against 28--42\% for the end-to-end policy (\cref{sec:failure_modes}). It did not raise full-task completion: the best subtask model places all three oranges in only 33/100 episodes, below the 41/100 LightwheelAI baseline, and on Teleop+Auto the monotask model leads on full-task success (18\% vs.\ 13\%) but not on mean occupancy (1.29 vs.\ 1.42). Decomposition secures more oranges on average, not more finished tasks.

\textbf{The grasp is the only hard step, and decomposition does not make it easier.} Once an orange is held the rest is reliable: lift and place each succeed 80--92\% of the time, and a secured orange reaches the plate 73--87\% of the time. The limiting step is acquisition --- only about 37\% of grasp attempts secure an orange, and the policy grasps the \emph{requested} orange just 67--73\% of the time, worst on \emph{right} (\cref{sec:obedience}). The orchestrator's checkpoints sit at boundaries that are already dependable; no amount of monitoring places an orange the policy never grasps. The three-way GRASP/LIFT/PLACE split is thus finer than the failure structure warrants: \textbf{Level-1} local retry almost never decides an outcome --- only about 6--11\% of placed oranges took more than one grasp --- so the realised contribution collapses onto Level-2 redirection alone, itself capped by how often a reachable alternative remains.

\textbf{Autonomous data helped one formulation and hurt the other.} Adding 414 self-generated successful subtasks \emph{helped} the monotask policy (full-task 14\% to 18\%, mean 1.02 to 1.29, empty episodes 44\% to 36\%) yet \emph{regressed} the orchestrated one (20\% to 13\%, mean 1.77 to 1.42, secured oranges per episode 2.13 to 1.94, a held orange placed 87\% down to 73\%). Why the same data helps one formulation and hurts the other is left open. One reading is that subtasking's advantage is redirection, which pays off only when the policy gets stuck: if the extra data made grasping more reliable, redirection would matter less and the formulation's minor overheads would dominate. Two observations keep this unresolved. First, the monotask's secured-per-episode rises on the merged data (1.26 to 1.47), weakly consistent with ``gets stuck less.'' Second, the orchestrated policy still fails completely far less often on Teleop+Auto (16\% vs.\ 36\% empty), so redirection is plainly still active --- arguing against the idea that it simply lost its purpose.

\textbf{More self-generated data is unlikely to be the fix.} The autonomous loop is a weak improvement mechanism by construction. SmolVLA produces each action chunk by integrating a flow-matching field from a Gaussian noise sample (\cref{subsec:finetuning}); trained by behavioural cloning on near-deterministic teleoperation, it largely reproduces the demonstrated motion rather than exploring alternative grasps. The harvested episodes are correspondingly narrow --- shorter and less dispersed than their Teleop counterparts (\cref{fig:dataset_composition}) --- a self-distribution that reinforces what the policy already does well. The loop also assigns a single success bit to a whole subtask with no within-chunk credit, a reward-weighted behavioural-cloning round rather than genuine policy search (\cref{subsec:autonomous}). A near-deterministic policy filtered by a coarse binary reward cannot discover the grasp it is missing; closing the acquisition gap likely needs explicit exploration --- for instance online reinforcement fine-tuning of the flow policy --- or targeted demonstrations on the hard \emph{right} and late-scene grasps, not more in-distribution data.

\textbf{Cheaper fine-tuning tweaks give no reliable gain.} Two alternatives to new data were tried. The LM-tuned variant (\cref{subsec:finetuning}) clearly helps only the Teleop subtask model (full-task 20\% to 33\%, mean 1.77 to 1.88); on the other three formulations the change is mixed and within sampling noise (Teleop monotask 14\% to 9\%, Teleop+Auto subtask 13\% to 10\%, Teleop+Auto monotask 18\% to 25\%). Removing the terminal freeze frames (\cref{subsec:teleop}), tried on the Teleop subtask alone, reaches 32\%/1.81 --- essentially the LM-tuned result. Because each action chunk is sampled from a fresh noise seed, differences of a few points over 100 episodes may reflect sampling noise rather than a real ordering; the one robust effect is that LM-tuning helps the Teleop subtask, and none of these variants approaches the 41\% monotask baseline.

\subsection{Conclusion}

This project asked whether a pick-and-place robot becomes more reliable when a controller is wrapped around its learned policy. Rather than run the whole task at once, the task is split into simple steps --- grasp an object, lift it, place it --- and after each step the controller checks whether it actually worked; when a step fails, the robot either retries the same object or gives up on it and turns to another, so the task can still make progress. This paid off in one clear way: the robot almost never ends empty-handed, because it can abandon an object it cannot pick up and still place a different one. It did not place all three objects more often, though. The reason is that the hardest part of the task is simply grasping the object the robot is asked for, and that step comes before any of the checks and recoveries can help. Wrapping the policy in a controller --- and even letting it practise and retrain on its own successful attempts --- leaves this bottleneck untouched: what the task most needs is a better grasping policy, not more structure around it.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{subsec:limitations}

Several limitations qualify these results. The broadest is interpretive: the system has many interacting design choices --- dataset composition, task formulation, which layers are fine-tuned, the per-subtask step budgets --- and only some are varied here, so the comparisons are best read as trends rather than as isolating a single decisive factor. Teasing each effect apart would take more experiments than this study runs.

\textbf{Privileged supervision.} Subtask outcomes are read from privileged simulator state, which a real deployment would have to replace with perception; the monotask grasp trace is reconstructed offline from that state, so its grasp attempts cannot be counted on the same footing as the orchestrated ones and no per-attempt monotask grasp \emph{rate} is reported.

\textbf{Limited sampling.} Each evaluation covers only 100 seeded episodes, so the reported percentages are limited-sample estimates and small differences may reflect sampling noise rather than a real effect.

\textbf{An unoptimised orchestrator.} The per-subtask step budgets and the finite-state machine itself are hand-set heuristics rather than optimised choices.

\textbf{Slow execution.} The teleoperated demonstrations were deliberately slow --- precise gamepad control demanded it (\cref{subsec:teleop}) --- so the policy inherited slow motions and each subtask consumes many simulation steps, which makes evaluation rollouts slow and the approach less suited to time-critical use.

\textbf{Chronologically constrained subtask data.} The subtasks were collected as isolated fragments in their natural chronological order, never starting a subtask from an out-of-sequence state. This was deliberate, so that the \emph{same} trajectories could serve the matched monotask comparison (\cref{subsec:monotask}), which is only meaningful for chronologically ordered steps --- being on one orange and then leaving it for another is senseless end-to-end. The effect is a policy whose behaviour stays entangled with apparent task progress rather than driven purely by the instruction: it follows the prompt only about two-thirds of the time (\cref{sec:obedience}) and cannot redirect from a state near an orange on its own, which is precisely why the scripted home-pose reset is required (\cref{subsec:failure_taxonomy}). Training on isolated fragments rather than complete, imperfect full-task trajectories also plausibly limits long-horizon chaining and off-distribution robustness. How a from-any-state dataset would lift these constraints is taken up in \cref{subsec:future_subtask}.

\subsection{Future Work}

The two levels of the system suggest two largely independent directions: making the atomistic subtask policy more capable, and generalising the orchestration around it.

\subsubsection{Subtask Execution}
\label{subsec:future_subtask}

The binding constraint --- grasp acquisition --- lies within the atomistic subtask policy, so making that policy more capable is the priority, and this is largely a matter of data. More demonstrations and higher-quality ones would help directly: the SO-101's five actuated arm joints are one short of the six degrees of freedom a full end-effector pose requires, which made hand-tracked teleoperation unintuitive and ultimately unusable (\cref{subsec:teleop}), so a 6-DOF arm would make teleoperation natural enough to collect a larger, more diverse, and cleaner dataset.

Quality also means \emph{coverage}. The chronologically-ordered data never exercises a subtask from an out-of-sequence state (\cref{subsec:limitations}); deliberately collecting such episodes --- being asked to grasp the right orange while already holding or hovering over another --- would make the policy a true from-any-state subtask executor that obeys the instruction from any configuration, and would let it redirect on its own without the scripted home-pose reset.

\subsubsection{Orchestration}

The high-level orchestration is largely orthogonal to the policy and can generalise along two axes. First, it currently reads each subtask's outcome from privileged simulator state, which is unavailable on a real robot (\cref{subsec:failure_taxonomy}); judging success from observations alone --- for instance with a vision-language model (VLM) as a success monitor --- would make the same control structure deployable in reality.

Second, the value of an explicit orchestrator should grow with task complexity. The present task is simple enough that a hand-designed finite-state machine suffices, but for long-horizon tasks with many object types, ordering constraints, and irreversible steps, enumerating and demonstrating every case end-to-end becomes infeasible --- exactly where a high-level controller earns its place. Given a reliable library of atomistic subtask policies, the decision of which subtask to run and when could be delegated either to a finite-state machine, when the task structure is known, or to an external language or vision-language model, when it is not, with the VLA optionally signalling subtask completion.
