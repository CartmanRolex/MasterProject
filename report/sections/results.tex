\section{Discussion \& Conclusion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{Decomposition broadened partial progress, not completion.} The hypothesis (\cref{sec:introduction}) was that wrapping the policy in subtask checkpoints with two-level recovery would make the long-horizon task more robust. The realised payoff is narrower: the orchestrated system almost never fails \emph{completely}, placing at least one orange in 97\% of Teleop episodes and 84\% on Teleop+Auto, against 57\% and 73\% for the monolithic policy. The mechanism is \textbf{Level-2} redirection --- giving up on an ungraspable orange to try another, about 2.7 distinct oranges per episode, which an end-to-end policy cannot do. It did not raise full-task completion: the best subtask model places all three oranges in only 20/100 episodes, below the 41/100 LightwheelAI baseline, and on Teleop+Auto the monolithic model is stronger on both full-task metrics. Decomposition secures more oranges on average, not more finished tasks.

\textbf{The grasp is the only hard step, and decomposition does not make it easier.} Once an orange is held the rest is reliable: lift and place each succeed 80--92\% of the time, and a secured orange reaches the plate 73--87\% of the time. The wall is acquisition --- only about 37\% of grasp attempts secure an orange, and the policy grasps the \emph{requested} orange just 67--73\% of the time, worst on \emph{right}, the label the task produces most (\cref{sec:obedience}). The orchestrator's checkpoints sit at boundaries that are already dependable; no amount of monitoring places an orange the policy never grasps. The three-way GRASP/LIFT/PLACE split is thus finer than the failure structure warrants: \textbf{Level-1} local retry almost never decides an outcome --- only 12 of 186 placed oranges took more than one grasp --- so the realised contribution collapses onto Level-2 redirection alone, itself capped by how often a reachable alternative remains.

\textbf{Autonomous data helped one formulation and hurt the other.} Adding 414 self-generated successful subtasks \emph{helped} the monolithic policy (full-task 18\% to 26\%, mean 1.11 to 1.50, empty episodes 43\% to 27\%) yet \emph{regressed} the orchestrated one (20\% to 13\%, mean 1.77 to 1.42, secured oranges per episode 2.13 to 1.94, a held orange placed 87\% down to 73\%). Why the same data helps one formulation and hurts the other is left open. One reading is that subtasking's advantage is redirection, which pays off only when the policy gets stuck: if the extra data made grasping more reliable, redirection would matter less and the formulation's minor overheads would dominate. Two observations keep this unresolved. First, the monolithic grasp-attempt count that would confirm ``gets stuck less'' is not yet measurable, though its secured-per-episode does rise (1.39 to 1.73). Second, the orchestrated policy still fails completely far less often on Teleop+Auto (16\% vs.\ 27\% empty), so redirection is plainly still active --- arguing against the idea that it simply lost its purpose.

\textbf{More self-generated data is unlikely to be the fix.} The autonomous loop is a weak improvement mechanism by construction. SmolVLA produces each action chunk by integrating a flow-matching field from a Gaussian noise sample (\cref{subsec:finetuning}); trained by behavioural cloning on near-deterministic teleoperation, it largely reproduces the demonstrated motion rather than exploring alternative grasps. The harvested episodes are correspondingly narrow --- shorter and less dispersed than their Teleop counterparts (\cref{fig:dataset_composition}) --- a self-distribution that reinforces what the policy already does well. The loop also assigns a single success bit to a whole subtask with no within-chunk credit, a reward-weighted behavioural-cloning round rather than genuine policy search (\cref{subsec:autonomous}). A near-deterministic policy filtered by a coarse binary reward cannot discover the grasp it is missing; closing the acquisition gap likely needs explicit exploration --- for instance online reinforcement fine-tuning of the flow policy --- or targeted demonstrations on the hard \emph{right} and late-scene grasps, not more in-distribution data.

\textbf{Cheaper recipe tweaks give no reliable gain.} Two alternatives to new data were tried. Unfreezing the language backbone (\cref{subsec:finetuning}) clearly helps only the Teleop subtask model (full-task 20\% to 33\%, mean 1.77 to 1.88); on the other three formulations it is neutral or worse (Teleop monotask 18\% to 9\%, Teleop+Auto subtask 13\% to 10\%, Teleop+Auto monotask 26\% to 25\%). Removing the terminal freeze frames (\cref{subsec:teleop}), tried on the Teleop subtask alone, reaches 32\%/1.81 --- essentially the unfrozen result. Because each action chunk is sampled from a fresh noise seed, differences of a few points over 100 episodes may reflect sampling noise rather than a real ordering; the one robust effect is that unfreezing helps the Teleop subtask, and none of these variants approaches the 41\% monolithic baseline.

\textbf{Limitations.} Subtask outcomes are read from privileged simulator state, which a deployment would have to replace with perception. Each evaluation is 100 seeded episodes, so the percentages are limited-sample estimates. The per-subtask step budgets that declare a timeout are hand-set and interact with the tail-freeze training (\cref{subsec:teleop}): a policy taught to idle once an orange is placed can exhaust the budget with the orange already in the plate, inflating apparent timeouts (most visibly the Teleop+Auto subtask) and arguing for retraining on a tail-free dataset. The monolithic grasp trace is reconstructed offline from privileged state, so no monolithic grasp rate or attempt count is reported --- the first planned upgrade is per-step gripper--orange proximity logging. Finally, neither model is trained on a complete, imperfect full-task trajectory (\cref{subsec:failure_taxonomy}): the monolithic data is relabelled subtask fragments and the Teleop subtasks were curated in isolation, which plausibly limits both long-horizon chaining and off-distribution robustness.

\textbf{Conclusion.} This project frames recovery as an execution-level requirement for VLA manipulation and separates it into two levels --- sub-goal recovery by Level-1 local retry and task recovery by Level-2 redirection --- wrapped around a SmolVLA subtask executor, with a scripted spatial reset as the enabler that lets language switch targets. The realised value of decomposition is robustness against \emph{total} failure: redirection lets the system abandon an ungraspable orange and still score, so it rarely ends empty. It did not raise full-task completion, because the binding constraint --- reliably acquiring the \emph{requested} orange --- lies upstream of every checkpoint and is untouched by the surrounding structure; neither the recovery levels nor the autonomous data removes it.

The lever is therefore the subtask policy, not the orchestration around it. The most promising next step is a more reliable grasp and target-selection policy --- through exploration-driven self-improvement such as online reinforcement fine-tuning of the flow policy, or targeted demonstrations on the worst targets --- together with some complete, imperfect full-task trajectories and the per-step proximity logging needed to characterise monolithic grasping. Only once acquisition is dependable is the orchestrator's two-level recovery likely to convert into higher full-task success.
