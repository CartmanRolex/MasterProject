\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

This work studies orchestrated manipulation in a simulated pick-and-place task. The robot is an SO-101 arm operating in an Isaac Sim kitchen scene. Each episode contains three oranges and a plate. The objective is to place all three oranges into the plate.

At every step the policy observes the same fixed inputs: the six SO-101 joint states and two RGB streams (a fixed front camera and a wrist camera). From these alone it predicts the next chunk of joint actions; it has no access to object coordinates or any privileged simulator quantity. Privileged state (orange and plate poses, contact forces) is used \emph{only} by the orchestrator and the evaluation harness to judge whether a subtask achieved its physical outcome. Withholding it from the policy avoids confounding orchestration with visual state-estimation errors, while exposing it to the orchestrator means subtask success is measured directly rather than inferred from perception.

\subsection{Policy Architecture and Fine-Tuning}
\label{subsec:finetuning}

The policy used throughout is \textbf{SmolVLA}~\cite{shukor2025smolvla}, a compact Vision-Language-Action model. It couples a pretrained vision-language backbone --- the \num{500}M-parameter \texttt{SmolVLM2-500M-Video-Instruct} (a vision encoder and a 16-layer language transformer) --- with a smaller \textbf{action expert}. The backbone encodes the two camera views and the prompt into tokens; the action expert cross-attends to them and, conditioned on the current joint state, denoises a noise sample into the next action chunk via a flow-matching objective, with a linear \textbf{state projection} mapping the six-dimensional joint state into token space. Each query outputs a chunk of 20 six-dimensional joint actions, matching the 20-frame terminal hold used in data collection (\cref{subsec:teleop}). All models start from the public \texttt{lerobot/smolvla\_base} checkpoint.

Fine-tuning is \textbf{parameter-efficient}: the vision encoder and the language backbone are frozen and only the \textbf{action expert} and \textbf{state projection} are updated --- roughly \num{100}M of the model's \num{450}M parameters (about 22\%) --- keeping the pretrained visual and linguistic priors intact while adapting the action pathway to the SO-101. Optimisation uses AdamW (learning rate \num{1e-4}, $\beta=(0.9,0.95)$, weight decay \num{1e-10}) on a cosine schedule with 1000-step warm-up, batch size 64, and \num{20000} gradient steps; the joint state and actions are mean/standard-deviation normalised and images identity-scaled. \Cref{fig:smolvla_finetune} summarises which components are frozen and which are trained.

As a separate ablation we \textbf{unfreeze the language backbone}, training the 16-layer transformer jointly with the action expert (the vision encoder stays frozen). This raises the trainable count from \num{100}M to \num{316}M (about 70\%) and lets the backbone adapt to the robot domain rather than reusing web-pretrained features, at the cost of a heavier optimisation more prone to overfitting a single dataset. It is trained on the same Teleop subtask data and evaluated under the identical protocol, so any difference is attributable to the unfreezing alone. On the 100-episode seeded benchmark of \cref{sec:experiments}, unfreezing raises full-task success (all three oranges placed) from 20\% to 33\% and the mean oranges placed from 1.77 to 1.88, while reaching success in fewer steps (2793 vs.\ 3043); the gain is concentrated in placement reliability, at the cost of a heavier tail of complete failures (9 vs.\ 3 zero-orange episodes). Unfreezing the backbone therefore helps on this single dataset, though the heavier optimisation makes the worst case more variable.

\subsection{LightwheelAI Baseline}

The first setting treats the task as a single full-task imitation problem. The \textbf{LightwheelAI Baseline} is the 60-episode \texttt{leisaac-pick-orange-mimic-v0} dataset published by LightwheelAI (developers of the upstream \texttt{leisaac} library this project builds on), the official reference for the task. Each of its 60 demonstrations covers the full three-orange sequence and is unsegmented --- not split into subtasks, with the policy expected to execute the whole task end-to-end.

Two policy classes are compared: ACT~\cite{zhao2023act}, an action-chunking transformer, and SmolVLA. Both are trained from the same 60-demonstration source under a single fixed instruction, so the comparison isolates the policy backbone rather than language conditioning. \textbf{[Placeholder: motivation for the ACT vs.\ SmolVLA comparison to be finalised.]} SmolVLA is used throughout the rest of the work. This experiment also establishes a performance ceiling for the \textbf{monotask} formulation --- the whole task run end-to-end under one prompt, without subtask checkpoints --- which we contrast throughout with the orchestrated \textbf{subtask} formulation: if even the stronger policy cannot solve the task reliably here, the limit is unlikely to be the low-level policy's expressiveness alone, motivating the decomposition introduced next.

\subsection{Subtask Decomposition}

Detecting and responding to intermediate failures requires knowing when a behaviour has ended and whether it succeeded. The full task is therefore reformulated as a sequence of subtasks --- grasping a selected orange, lifting it, and placing it --- creating explicit checkpoints where the expected physical outcome can be verified before committing to the next step.

Targeted grasp prompts use relative spatial labels from the current layout: left, middle, right in simple configurations. When two oranges are too close along the main horizontal axis to separate by left/right alone, the secondary axis breaks the tie (labels such as top, bottom, top right, bottom left), keeping the language target meaningful even when oranges are laterally clustered.

An intermediate attempt sliced the LightwheelAI Baseline into subtask segments, but its demonstrations always follow the same object order, so target identity is entangled with progress through the fixed sequence rather than tied to the instruction. This motivated collecting a custom dataset.

\subsection{Teleop Dataset Construction}
\label{subsec:teleop}

A custom teleoperated dataset, referred to as \textbf{Teleop}, was collected to support orchestrated execution, with the SO-101 arm driven by hand through an Xbox gamepad controller. Each episode is a single successful subtask annotated with its instruction, such as ``Grasp left orange'', ``Grasp top right orange'', ``Pick it up'', or ``Place it into plate''. The pick order is randomised across episodes, which breaks the fixed-order correlation of the baseline and forces the policy to associate a relative-position instruction with the specified orange rather than inferring the target from task progress. The dataset used for the evaluated subtask model contains 846 such episodes.

The teleoperated motions are markedly slower than the baseline: precise fast control is hard with the gamepad, so the operator moved deliberately. The trajectories therefore differ from the LightwheelAI Baseline in velocity profile, not only in subtask-level segmentation.

A key design choice is \textbf{tailing}: every episode is extended with 20 terminal freeze frames (one action chunk), repeating the final observation and state and converting the command into a hold once the subtask completes. This teaches the policy to \textbf{stay idle at a subtask boundary} instead of flowing into the next phase, which serves three roles. It lets a manually-driven operator pause and think while the arm holds its pose and keeps its grasp instead of drifting or dropping the orange. More importantly, it makes the policy robust to an orchestrator that does not advance instantaneously --- e.g.\ a higher-latency external vision-based controller --- since it holds rather than drifts while waiting. And because a finished subtask manifests as the arm coming to rest, the tail gives a natural completion signal a higher-level controller can detect. The resulting SmolVLA model is thus a subtask executor: on reaching a terminal configuration it settles and waits rather than continuing to act.

\subsection{Orchestrator Design}
\label{subsec:orchestrator}

The orchestrator is a finite-state controller wrapping the VLA, which executes only three prompts: GRASP(\textit{target}), LIFT, and PLACE. The controller chooses the target, sends the prompt, monitors the physical outcome, and decides whether to advance, retry, redirect, or terminate --- using privileged state, so the experiment evaluates the control structure rather than visual state estimation.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{orchestrator_decision_flow.pdf}
    \caption{Decision logic of the orchestrated evaluator. The VLA is queried only for manipulation subtasks; target selection, local retries, target redirection, and the home-pose reset are handled by the controller.}
    \label{fig:orchestrator_flow}
\end{figure}

At the start and after each placement the controller enters \texttt{SELECT\_TARGET}: it reads orange positions, drops those already in the plate, assigns relative labels, and picks the next GRASP target (the episode ends when none remain). After a GRASP timeout it records that orange as the last failed target and avoids it while another is available --- a switch counted as \textbf{target redirection}.

Each subtask is guarded by an explicit physical check. \textbf{GRASP} is confirmed when the gripper is centred on an unplaced orange, fingertips closed, with contact force on both gripper contacts over consecutive frames; if a \emph{different} orange is grasped, that one becomes the active target. \textbf{LIFT} is confirmed when the active orange stays held and rises at least \SI{0.06}{m} above its start height. \textbf{PLACE} is confirmed when it rests inside the oriented plate bounds, stable over consecutive frames, with the gripper open.

Timeouts are 700 steps for GRASP, 400 for LIFT, and 500 for PLACE. A confirmed GRASP advances to LIFT, LIFT to PLACE, and PLACE returns to \texttt{SELECT\_TARGET} for the next orange (or ends the episode once all three are placed).

\textbf{Recovery is defined at two levels of the goal hierarchy} --- the task (place all three oranges) and the per-orange goal (GRASP\,$\rightarrow$\,LIFT\,$\rightarrow$\,PLACE for one orange). Pinning each recovery action to a level keeps the term precise and applies identically to both formulations. The orchestrator implements one mechanism per level plus a precondition that enables the higher one; the three must not be conflated.

\textbf{Local retry is Level-1 (sub-goal) recovery}: it recovers the \emph{same} orange. On a drop during LIFT/PLACE, or a LIFT/PLACE timeout, the controller keeps the target and returns directly to GRASP --- the sub-goal is still active, with no target-change reset.

\textbf{Target redirection is Level-2 (task) recovery}: it recovers task \emph{progress} by switching to a \emph{different} orange. After a GRASP timeout, if another unplaced orange is available, the controller deliberately picks a different one so the episode keeps scoring instead of stalling on an unreachable target. The sub-goal is dropped, but the objective --- indifferent to \emph{which} orange is placed --- is served, so this is task-level recovery, not mere abandonment. If no alternative remains, it retries the same orange (a Level-1 retry). Redirection is tracked as a counter; oranges are not permanently marked abandoned in the final metrics.

\textbf{Home-pose reset is a precondition, not a recovery level.} It returns the arm to the kind of \emph{high, far-from-the-oranges configuration} it can be re-targeted from --- a property of our data, not of VLAs in general. Such configurations appear at only two moments: episode start and just after a placement, when the gripper has retracted to select the next orange. There the dataset shows the \emph{same} pose branching to \emph{different} oranges by instruction, so the policy learns the language-to-target choice; once the gripper is low and near an orange there is no such branch, because grasps were demonstrated one target at a time, and a new prompt is ignored (quantified in \cref{sec:obedience}). The reset reproduces a high, far configuration, the only region where that mapping was trained. We prefer it to collecting redirection demonstrations for a second reason: the monotask models reuse the \emph{same} trajectories under one instruction and need only finish the episode, so redirection episodes would have to be filtered out of that shared data, weakening the controlled comparison. On a GRASP timeout the controller clears the target, bypasses the VLA, and drives the arm back along a short scripted trajectory --- first raising the gripper cleanly upward, then returning to the start joint configuration, so it disengages without disturbing the other oranges --- after which the action queue is cleared and target selection resumes. A Level-1 retry on the same target after a slip needs no reset.

\subsection{Subtask Outcomes and Failure Modes}
\label{subsec:failure_taxonomy}

Each subtask has one intended outcome, confirmed by the checks of \cref{subsec:orchestrator}; a failure is the negation of that check, in one of three physical modes. A \textbf{grasp failure}: the target is never acquired (the gripper never closes on it with confirmed contact). A \textbf{lift failure}: an acquired orange never rises \SI{0.06}{m} above its start height, or slips during the lift. A \textbf{place failure}: a lifted orange never comes to rest inside the plate, or is dropped outside on release. Read directly from simulator state, all three are well defined for any rollout, independent of task framing.

What differs between formulations is how failures are detected and what follows. Under the subtask formulation each subtask uses the step budget above and is declared failed when its check is unmet within it. The timeout is thus a \emph{detection trigger}, not a fourth failure mode: it lets the orchestrator notice the failure and respond with one of the mechanisms of \cref{subsec:orchestrator}. GRASP can only ever fail by timeout --- no instantaneous event marks a grasp impossible --- whereas LIFT and PLACE can also fail the moment the orange is lost.

This mapping onto the two recovery levels is what makes the recovery metrics of \cref{sec:experiments} fair across formulations. A \textbf{LIFT drop} or \textbf{PLACE slip} happens to an orange that was demonstrably held, so its identity is read from contact and ``the same orange'' is unambiguous for both formulations --- the natural trigger for \textbf{Level-1} (same-orange) recovery. A \textbf{GRASP failure} never establishes contact, so physics gives no orange identity, and attributing it to an intended target would require guessing the monotask trace cannot do reliably; this dead-end is what \textbf{Level-2} (different-orange) recovery addresses, and Level-2 needs no attribution --- it only asks whether a \emph{different} orange is later placed. Scoring each failure where the worked orange is unambiguous lets both formulations use the same physical reads, with no inferred targets.

The monotask formulation has no such structure: the policy runs end-to-end under one global prompt within a single 5000-step budget, with no checkpoints and no per-subtask timeout. The same three failure modes occur but are never detected or acted on online. For a common footing, the analysis in \cref{sec:experiments} reconstructs the same physical events for monotask runs offline from privileged state (inferring the worked target from the layout), so grasp, lift, and place outcomes are readable for both formulations even though only the orchestrated one observes them online.

\subsection{Autonomous Data Generation}

Autonomous data generation uses the orchestrated system to collect training data without teleoperation. It was motivated by the first evaluation of the Teleop subtask model: subtasking plus orchestration did not beat the SmolVLA monotask baseline on full-task success, suggesting the subtask policy needed more data --- especially from the states it visits during autonomous execution, not only manual demonstrations.

A rollout can contain both failed and successful subtasks; the system keeps the successful trajectories and discards the failed ones, yielding a dataset of successful subtasks under the policy's own execution distribution --- the balanced retained subset is \textbf{Auto}. Target redirection matters here: without pivoting to a different orange after a GRASP timeout, an entire rollout would be discarded, so redirection lets the system keep collecting from scenes that would otherwise be wasted.

\textbf{A reinforcement-learning reading.} This filter-and-retrain loop can be viewed as a rudimentary form of reinforcement learning. The task offers no dense reward to shape behaviour; instead, the orchestrator's privileged success checks provide a \emph{sparse, binary} one --- a subtask trajectory earns reward 1 if it reaches its intended physical outcome and 0 otherwise. Rolling the current policy out on freshly randomised scenes, keeping only the rewarded trajectories, and fine-tuning on them is then a single iteration of reward-filtered self-imitation: it is equivalent to reward-weighted behavioural cloning in which the weight is simply the success indicator. The scheme is deliberately minimal. Because the reward is a single bit for an entire subtask, it performs no temporal credit assignment within the trajectory: every transition of a retained subtask is treated as equally good, and no action is up- or down-weighted relative to the others as a true advantage estimate would be. It is thus the crudest form of policy improvement from self-generated experience --- keep the successes, discard the failures, and clone --- with no value function, no advantage, and a single update round. That simplicity is precisely why it was worth trying first: it reuses the existing orchestrator and imitation-learning pipeline unchanged, and only if this cheapest possible data loop helped would a more elaborate weighting be justified.

The generated episodes were then filtered to balance by scene state --- equalising episodes collected with one or two oranges already placed against start-of-episode ones, so the set does not over-represent early scenes. The 414 retained Auto episodes were merged with the 846 Teleop episodes into \textbf{Teleop+Auto} (1260 episodes), on which the Teleop+Auto subtask model was trained.

\subsection{Monotask Fair Comparison}

A subtask model differs from the LightwheelAI Baseline in both training distribution and formulation, so that comparison cannot isolate subtasking. We therefore pair each subtask model with a \textbf{monotask} counterpart trained on the \emph{same} trajectories: per-subtask instructions replaced by the single fixed prompt ``Place the orange into plate'', freeze tails removed, run end-to-end without the orchestrator's checkpoints. The pair sees identical demonstrations and differs only in framing, at both training and execution time; every run uses the same 5000-step budget.

The match is in data but not temporal supervision --- intentionally, since supervision is part of the formulation under test: the freeze tails teach the subtask model to settle at each boundary, while the monotask model never sees boundaries and keeps acting under one prompt. The results thus compare two formulations, not a single controlled factor.

A further caveat: neither model is trained on a genuine end-to-end three-orange trajectory. The monotask data is itself reused subtask fragments under one prompt, not continuous runs, and the Teleop subtasks were curated for quality in isolation --- a coverage gap discussed in \cref{sec:discussion}.
