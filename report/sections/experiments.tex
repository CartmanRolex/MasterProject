\section{Results}
\label{sec:experiments}

This section reports the experimental evidence collected so far. The emphasis is on factual comparisons between task formulations and training sources. The dataset names used throughout are: \textbf{LightwheelAI Baseline} for the 60-episode \texttt{leisaac-pick-orange-mimic-v0} demonstrations published by LightwheelAI, \textbf{Teleop} for the manually collected subtask dataset, \textbf{Auto} for the balanced automatically generated successful subtasks, and \textbf{Teleop+Auto} for their merged training set.

The current evaluations use the same reference seed set, \texttt{pick\_orange\_reference\_100\_v1}, for every completed model run. Each run therefore contains the same 100 initial conditions in the same order; across seed indices, the randomized quantities are the top-camera pose and the orange positions. This makes differences between bars more directly attributable to the policy and task formulation rather than to easier or harder sampled scenes.

\subsection{Full-Task Outcomes Across Formulations}

\Cref{fig:orange_outcome_distribution} reports final plate occupancy for every evaluated model on the shared 100-seed reference set, under an identical 5000-step episode budget. Two complementary metrics are read from it. The \emph{full-task success rate} is the fraction of episodes ending with all three oranges in the plate; it measures whether the long-horizon task is actually solved. The \emph{mean final occupancy} is the average number of oranges placed out of three; it credits partial progress and so separates models that make headway from those that stall early. Bars are grouped by training source --- LightwheelAI Baseline, Teleop, and Teleop+Auto --- and, within the two custom sources, by task formulation: a \emph{monotask} policy run end-to-end versus an orchestrated \emph{subtask} policy.

\paragraph{Subtasking improves over monotasking.} On the same training trajectories, the orchestrated subtask formulation outperforms the monotask one on both metrics. The effect is clearest on Teleop, where decomposition roughly doubles full-task success (13\% to 29\%) and lifts mean occupancy from 0.92 to 1.80, indicating that explicit subtask checkpoints convert otherwise-stalled episodes into progress. On Teleop+Auto the advantage narrows and splits between the two metrics: the subtask model retains a slight edge in mean occupancy (1.47 vs.\ 1.42) but a lower full-task success rate (13\% vs.\ 26\%). Decomposition therefore reliably helps a policy make partial progress, but on the merged data it does not translate that progress into complete-task success --- the controller secures more oranges on average yet less often finishes all three.

\paragraph{Subtasking rarely fails completely.} The gap is most striking on the weakest outcome --- ending an episode with no orange placed at all. The orchestrated subtask models almost always place at least one orange (89\% of episodes on Teleop, 84\% on Teleop+Auto), whereas the monotask models leave a large fraction of episodes empty (only 50\% reach one orange on Teleop, 69\% on Teleop+Auto). A plausible explanation lies in the orchestrator's target redirection: a monotask policy that fixates on a single hard-to-grasp orange can burn the entire episode without ever placing one, while the controller detects a repeated GRASP timeout, abandons that orange, and redirects to another. The decomposed formulation thus rarely produces a total failure, even when it does not complete the full task.

\paragraph{Aggregating autonomous data did not help.} Adding the automatically generated episodes to Teleop did not strengthen the policy. Under the orchestrated subtask formulation it in fact regressed performance on both metrics relative to Teleop alone (full-task success 29\% to 13\%, mean occupancy 1.80 to 1.47). Augmenting the dataset with in-distribution successful subtasks was thus not sufficient to improve full-task behaviour; the subtask-level analysis below isolates which behaviours degraded.

\subsection{Within-Episode Behaviour: Lift Recovery and Target Obedience}
\label{sec:within_episode}

The full-task outcomes above measure what each policy achieves, not how it behaves while attempting the task. This section reconstructs two within-episode behaviours from the per-step physics traces: how policies respond when a grasped orange slips during the lift, and --- for the orchestrated runs --- whether a positional grasp instruction is actually obeyed. The four models compared are the two task formulations (monotask versus orchestrated subtask) on each of the two custom training sets (Teleop and Teleop+Auto). The two monotask traces are observer-inferred from physics state alone (the end-to-end policy exposes no intent), whereas the subtask traces are the orchestrator's own ground-truth subtask log; comparisons across formulations should be read with that asymmetry in mind.

\paragraph{Lift failures and recovery.} A \emph{lift} begins once a grasped orange is held and rises above its initial height; it \emph{drops} if the orange leaves the gripper before reaching the plate. \Cref{tab:lift_recovery} reports the per-attempt lift success rate (successes over successes plus drops, excluding attempts truncated by episode end), and what happens after a drop. Two recovery quantities are distinguished following the project's mechanism vocabulary: whether a dropped orange is \emph{eventually} lifted later in the same episode, and whether the \emph{next} lift attempt re-engages the \emph{same} orange that was just dropped --- the signature of a local retry, as opposed to abandoning that orange for another.

The dominant effect is not the raw lift success rate --- which is comparable across formulations except for the weak Teleop monotask model --- but what happens after a drop. The orchestrated subtask policies re-engage the dropped orange far more often than the monotask policies (83.9\% vs.\ 50.0\% on Teleop; 63.2\% vs.\ 25.0\% on Teleop+Auto), and correspondingly recover a much larger share of dropped oranges (72.7\% vs.\ 26.0\%; 38.5\% vs.\ 14.3\%). This is the local-retry mechanism made visible: when an orange slips during the lift, the orchestrator returns to GRASP for the \emph{same} orange, whereas the monotask policy, having no such mechanism, tends to drift to a different orange and the dropped one is rarely reclaimed. When recovery does occur it is almost always immediate --- on average a single retry separates the drop from the eventual successful lift in every model --- so the recovery gap is driven by \emph{whether} the same orange is re-attempted at all, not by how persistently it is retried. The contrast is sharpest on Teleop+Auto, where the monotask policy abandons three out of four dropped oranges.

\paragraph{Obedience to positional grasp instructions.} The orchestrated runs request a specific orange by spatial label (e.g.\ ``grasp the right orange''), so we can ask how often the policy grasps the orange it was told to. Obedience is measured only over grasps that actually engaged an orange --- timeouts and empty grasps are failures to grasp, not wrong-orange grasps --- and counts a grasp as obedient when the grasped orange is the requested one by identity, not merely by label.

A measurement caveat constrains the positional breakdown. The spatial labels are recomputed live at each target selection, but always over all three oranges, including those already resting in the plate. Once oranges are placed, the remaining labels therefore degenerate: with two oranges placed, the single remaining orange is labelled ``right'' in 100\% of cases, because the two plated oranges occupy the other slots. Aggregating over all grasps thus inflates obedience with trivially unambiguous late-episode requests. We therefore restrict the per-position breakdown in \Cref{tab:obedience} to the opening grasp of each episode, when all three oranges are genuinely on the table and the labels carry real positional meaning.

Overall obedience on genuinely ambiguous scenes is only about two-thirds (65.4\% Teleop, 69.0\% Teleop+Auto), well below the figure obtained when the degenerate late-episode requests are included. The error is strongly directional and consistent across both training sets: requests for the \emph{right} orange are obeyed only 24--32\% of the time, and \emph{bottom right} 53--64\%, whereas \emph{left} and \emph{top right} are obeyed 88--97\%. The policy thus reliably resolves some spatial referents but systematically mishandles others, grasping a neighbouring orange instead. This is direct evidence that the VLA's language conditioning is an imperfect and spatially biased channel --- the same context-dependence that motivates the scripted spatial reset --- rather than a clean selector that the orchestrator can assume is obeyed.
