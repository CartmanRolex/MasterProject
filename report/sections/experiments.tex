\section{Results}
\label{sec:experiments}

This section reports the experimental evidence collected so far. The emphasis is on factual comparisons between task formulations and training sources. The dataset names used throughout are: \textbf{LightwheelAI Baseline} for the 60-episode \texttt{leisaac-pick-orange-mimic-v0} demonstrations published by LightwheelAI, \textbf{Teleop} for the manually collected subtask dataset, \textbf{Auto} for the balanced automatically generated successful subtasks, and \textbf{Teleop+Auto} for their merged training set.

The current evaluations use the same reference seed set, \texttt{pick\_orange\_reference\_100\_v1}, for every completed model run. Each run therefore contains the same 100 initial conditions in the same order; across seed indices, the randomized quantities are the top-camera pose and the orange positions. This makes differences between bars more directly attributable to the policy and task formulation rather than to easier or harder sampled scenes.

\subsection{Full-Task Outcomes Across Formulations}

\Cref{fig:orange_outcome_distribution} reports final plate occupancy for every evaluated model on the shared 100-seed reference set, under an identical 5000-step episode budget. Two complementary metrics are read from it. The \emph{full-task success rate} is the fraction of episodes ending with all three oranges in the plate; it measures whether the long-horizon task is actually solved. The \emph{mean final occupancy} is the average number of oranges placed out of three; it credits partial progress and so separates models that make headway from those that stall early. Bars are grouped by training source --- LightwheelAI Baseline, Teleop, and Teleop+Auto --- and, within the two custom sources, by task formulation: a monolithic \emph{monotask} policy run end-to-end versus an orchestrated \emph{subtask} policy.

\paragraph{Subtasking improves over monotasking.} On the same training trajectories, the orchestrated subtask formulation outperforms the monolithic one on both metrics. The effect is clearest on Teleop, where decomposition roughly doubles full-task success (13\% to 29\%) and lifts mean occupancy from 0.92 to 1.80, indicating that explicit subtask checkpoints convert otherwise-stalled episodes into progress. On Teleop+Auto the advantage narrows and splits between the two metrics: the subtask model retains a slight edge in mean occupancy (1.47 vs.\ 1.42) but a lower full-task success rate (13\% vs.\ 26\%). Decomposition therefore reliably helps a policy make partial progress, but on the merged data it does not translate that progress into complete-task success --- the controller secures more oranges on average yet less often finishes all three.

\paragraph{Subtasking rarely fails completely.} The gap is most striking on the weakest outcome --- ending an episode with no orange placed at all. The orchestrated subtask models almost always place at least one orange (89\% of episodes on Teleop, 84\% on Teleop+Auto), whereas the monotask models leave a large fraction of episodes empty (only 50\% reach one orange on Teleop, 69\% on Teleop+Auto). A plausible explanation lies in the orchestrator's target redirection: a monolithic policy that fixates on a single hard-to-grasp orange can burn the entire episode without ever placing one, while the controller detects a repeated GRASP timeout, abandons that orange, and redirects to another. The decomposed formulation thus rarely produces a total failure, even when it does not complete the full task.

\paragraph{Aggregating autonomous data did not help.} Adding the automatically generated episodes to Teleop did not strengthen the policy. Under the orchestrated subtask formulation it in fact regressed performance on both metrics relative to Teleop alone (full-task success 29\% to 13\%, mean occupancy 1.80 to 1.47). Augmenting the dataset with in-distribution successful subtasks was thus not sufficient to improve full-task behaviour; the subtask-level analysis below isolates which behaviours degraded.

\subsection{Subtask-Level Outcomes}

Table~\ref{tab:orchestrated_subtasks} reports success rates by subtask and by how many oranges were already in the plate at subtask start.

For the Teleop model, GRASP is the lowest-success subtask and decreases as the episode progresses from 0 to 2 oranges already placed. LIFT and PLACE are higher once an orange has been acquired. In the Teleop+Auto run, GRASP is lower for scenes with 0 or 1 orange already placed, and PLACE is lower across all scene states. This explains why adding Auto data did not help: the added data did not improve the bottleneck behaviours that determine whether the orchestrator can actually make progress.

The orchestrator mechanisms fired frequently in both orchestrated evaluations. In the Teleop run, local retries occurred in 68/100 episodes and target redirections in 77/100 episodes. In the Teleop+Auto run, local retries occurred in 80/100 episodes and target redirections in 89/100 episodes.
