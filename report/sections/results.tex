\section{Discussion \& Conclusion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{The limitation is structural, not just the policy class.} Even the stronger SmolVLA monotask baseline solves only 41/100 episodes, so changing the policy backbone alone does not make the long-horizon task reliable. The monotask formulation gives the policy no point at which an intermediate outcome is verified: a local failure simply continues under the same global prompt, plausibly leaving the scene in a rarely-seen state from which behaviour degrades --- a hypothesis (\cref{sec:introduction}), not a demonstrated mechanism.

\textbf{Decomposition creates real intervention points, but their payoff is narrow.} What the subtask checkpoints buy is the ability to give up on a hard orange and try another: the orchestrated model attempts about 2.7 distinct oranges per episode, which the end-to-end policy cannot do (\cref{sec:failure_modes}). The sliced-baseline experiment showed checkpoints alone are not enough, though: this redirection also needs the ability to request a specific orange --- which a fixed-order dataset cannot teach and the Teleop dataset was built to provide.

\textbf{Neither level substitutes for a reliable subtask policy.} The Teleop subtask model reaches 20/100 full-task success (mean 1.77/3) --- above its monotask pair (18/100, 1.11) but still below the 41/100 baseline. The bottleneck is GRASP: the policy follows the spatial instruction only about two-thirds to three-quarters of the time --- worst on \emph{right}, the very target the task produces most (\cref{sec:obedience}) --- and explicit monitoring cannot place an orange the policy never grasps.

\textbf{Autonomous data did not fix this.} Adding 414 Auto subtasks \emph{reduced} orchestrated performance (13/100, mean 1.42); on the merged data the monotask is in fact stronger on both full-task metrics (26/100, 1.50), and the subtask's redirection edge shrinks accordingly (\cref{sec:failure_modes}). Tellingly, this happened even though the per-subtask policy was, if anything, slightly \emph{more} obedient with the extra data (\cref{sec:obedience}): the limit is the reliability of the subtask policy --- above all selecting the requested orange --- not the amount of in-distribution data.

\textbf{Limitations.} Success checks use privileged simulator state, which deployment would have to replace with perception. Each completed evaluation is 100 seeded episodes, so the exact percentages are limited-sample estimates. The Auto set contains only successful subtasks from a weak policy, so it may reinforce a narrow visited-state distribution rather than broaden it. The per-subtask step budgets that declare a subtask timed-out are also somewhat arbitrary, and they interact with the tail-freeze training (\cref{subsec:teleop}): a subtask policy taught to idle once an orange is held or placed sometimes exhausts the budget while the orange is already in the plate. Place success is read from the orange's final position rather than the orchestrator's confirmation, so these cases count as successes, but they inflate the apparent per-subtask timeout counts (most visibly the Teleop+Auto subtask) and argue for retraining on a \emph{tail-free} dataset, where completion is signalled by the policy coming to rest rather than by a fixed budget. The monotask grasp trace is also reconstructed heuristically (its target is re-inferred every few frames), so we deliberately report no monotask grasp rate or attempt count, and cannot yet test whether the end-to-end policy \emph{fixates} on a single orange --- that needs per-step gripper--orange proximity logging, the first planned upgrade to the evaluation. Finally, neither model is trained on a complete episode (\cref{subsec:failure_taxonomy}): the ``full-task'' monotask data is relabelled subtask fragments and the Teleop subtasks were curated in isolation, so the data contains no full successful run, nor the imperfect mid-episode states a policy actually visits, which plausibly limits both long-horizon chaining and off-distribution robustness.

\textbf{Conclusion.} This project frames recovery as an execution-level requirement for VLA-based manipulation and separates it into two levels --- sub-goal recovery by local retry and task recovery by redirection. Decomposition demonstrably creates the intervention points --- a confirmed grasp converts to a placement more reliably on Teleop --- but the implemented system does not yet deliver full-task robustness: the mechanisms cannot compensate for an insufficiently reliable subtask policy, and adding autonomous data hurt rather than helped. The most useful next step is therefore not more autonomous data but a more reliable subtask policy --- especially selecting the requested orange and late-scene PLACE --- and collecting some complete, imperfect full-task trajectories, before expecting orchestration to raise full-task success.
