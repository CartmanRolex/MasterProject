\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

This work studies orchestrated manipulation in a simulated pick-and-place task. The robot is an SO-101 arm operating in an Isaac Sim kitchen scene. Each episode contains three oranges and a plate. The objective is to place all three oranges into the plate.

At every step, the learned policy observes the same fixed set of inputs: the six joint states of the SO-101 arm and two RGB camera streams, a fixed front camera viewing the workspace and a wrist camera mounted on the gripper. From these observations alone the policy predicts the next chunk of joint actions; it has no access to object coordinates or any privileged simulator quantity. Privileged simulator state (orange and plate poses, contact forces) is used \emph{only} by the orchestrator and the evaluation harness to decide whether a subtask achieved its intended physical outcome. Keeping this state out of the policy input avoids confounding the orchestration problem with visual state-estimation errors, while keeping it available to the orchestrator means subtask success is measured directly rather than inferred from perception.

\subsection{Policy Architecture and Fine-Tuning}
\label{subsec:finetuning}

The learned policy used throughout this work is \textbf{SmolVLA}, a compact Vision-Language-Action model. It couples a pretrained vision-language backbone --- the \num{500}-million-parameter \texttt{SmolVLM2-500M-Video-Instruct} model, comprising a vision encoder and a 16-layer language transformer --- with a smaller \textbf{action expert}. At each step the backbone encodes the two camera views and the language prompt into a sequence of tokens; the action expert cross-attends to those tokens and, conditioned on the current joint state, denoises a noise sample into the next action chunk through a flow-matching objective. A separate linear \textbf{state projection} maps the six-dimensional joint state into the model's token space. The policy outputs a chunk of 20 six-dimensional joint actions per query, matching the 20-frame terminal hold used during data collection (\cref{subsec:teleop}). All models are initialised from the public \texttt{lerobot/smolvla\_base} checkpoint and fine-tuned on our datasets.

Fine-tuning follows a \textbf{parameter-efficient} scheme in which the bulk of the pretrained backbone is kept frozen. The vision encoder is frozen, so the perceptual features are reused as-is; the language transformer of the backbone is also frozen; and only the \textbf{action expert} and the \textbf{state projection} are updated. Under this scheme roughly \num{100} million of the model's \num{450} million parameters are trainable (about 22\%). This keeps the broad visual and linguistic priors of the pretrained model intact while adapting the action-generation pathway to the SO-101 and the task. All models are optimised with AdamW (learning rate \num{1e-4}, $\beta=(0.9,0.95)$, weight decay \num{1e-10}) under a cosine schedule with a 1000-step warm-up, a batch size of 64, and \num{20000} gradient steps. Observations are normalised with image identity scaling and mean/standard-deviation normalisation for the joint state and actions. \Cref{fig:smolvla_finetune} summarises which components are frozen and which are trained.

\subsection{LightwheelAI Baseline}

The first experimental setting treats the task as a single full-task imitation-learning problem. This report refers to this training source as the \textbf{LightwheelAI Baseline} dataset. It is the 60-episode \texttt{leisaac-pick-orange-mimic-v0} dataset published by LightwheelAI, the developers of the upstream \texttt{leisaac} library this project builds on, and serves as the official reference dataset for the task. It contains 60 complete pick-and-place demonstrations, where each episode covers the full sequence of picking the three oranges and placing them in the plate. The demonstrations are unsegmented: they are not split into subtasks, and the learned policy is expected to execute the full task from start to finish.

Two policy classes are compared: ACT, an action-chunking transformer policy, and SmolVLA, a Vision-Language-Action model. Both are trained from the same 60-demonstration full-task source, using compatible LeRobot dataset versions, and both receive a single fixed instruction for the whole episode, so the comparison isolates the policy backbone rather than any language conditioning. \textbf{[Placeholder: motivation for the ACT vs.\ SmolVLA comparison to be finalised.]} SmolVLA is the backbone used throughout the rest of this work, since the goal of the project is to study orchestration on top of a VLA. Independently of the comparison, this experiment establishes a performance ceiling for the \textbf{monotask} formulation --- the whole task run end-to-end under a single prompt, without subtask checkpoints --- which this report contrasts throughout with the orchestrated \textbf{subtask} formulation. If even the stronger policy cannot solve the full task reliably under this monotask setting, the limitation is unlikely to be the expressiveness of the low-level policy alone, which motivates the subtask decomposition and orchestration introduced next.

\subsection{Subtask Decomposition}

Detecting and responding to intermediate failures requires knowing when one behaviour has ended and whether it succeeded. For this reason, the full task is reformulated as a sequence of shorter subtasks. The main subtasks are grasping a selected orange, lifting it, and placing it into the plate. These subtasks create explicit checkpoints where the execution system can evaluate whether the expected physical outcome has been achieved before committing to the next step.

Targeted grasp prompts use relative spatial labels computed from the current orange layout. In simple configurations, the oranges are labelled left, middle, and right. When two oranges are too close along the main horizontal axis to be reliably distinguished by only left/right position, the secondary horizontal axis is used as a tie-breaker, yielding labels such as top, bottom, top left, bottom left, top right, or bottom right. This keeps the language target meaningful even when the oranges are laterally clustered.

These subtask checkpoints are where a subtask outcome --- success or one of the physical failure modes --- is evaluated from privileged simulator state rather than inferred from the policy. The precise outcome and failure definitions, shared by both task formulations, are given in \cref{subsec:failure_taxonomy}.

An intermediate attempt was made by slicing the LightwheelAI Baseline dataset into shorter subtask segments. This produced a subtask-style dataset, but the original demonstrations always follow the same object order. As a result, the sliced data does not provide a meaningful signal connecting a language instruction to a specific target object: target identity is entangled with progress through the fixed sequence rather than with the instruction itself. This limitation motivated the creation of a custom dataset.

\subsection{Teleop Dataset Construction}
\label{subsec:teleop}

A custom teleoperated dataset, referred to as \textbf{Teleop}, was collected to support orchestrated execution, with the SO-101 arm driven by hand through an Xbox gamepad controller. Each episode is a single successful subtask annotated with its instruction, such as ``Grasp left orange'', ``Grasp top right orange'', ``Pick it up'', or ``Place it into plate''. The pick order is randomised across episodes, which breaks the fixed-order correlation of the baseline and forces the policy to associate a relative-position instruction with the specified orange rather than inferring the target from task progress. The dataset used for the evaluated subtask model contains 846 such episodes.

The teleoperated motions are markedly slower than the baseline demonstrations: precise yet fast control is difficult to achieve with the Xbox gamepad, so the operator favoured careful, deliberate movements. The resulting trajectories therefore differ in velocity profile from the LightwheelAI Baseline, not only in their subtask-level segmentation.

A key design choice is the \textbf{tailing of each subtask}: every episode is extended with 20 terminal freeze frames, corresponding to one action chunk in the evaluated setup, which repeat the terminal visual observation and robot state and convert the command into a hold-style action once the subtask is complete. The purpose of the tail is to \textbf{teach the policy to stay idle at the end of a subtask} instead of immediately flowing into the next manipulation phase. This idle-on-completion behaviour serves several roles. When prompts are inserted manually --- deciding the next subtask by hand while the policy runs --- it lets the operator pause and think without the arm doing something undesirable in the meantime: instead of drifting or dropping a held orange because it never learned to wait, the policy holds its terminal pose, keeping a firm grasp on the orange until the next instruction is issued. More importantly, it makes the policy robust to an orchestrator that does not advance instantaneously: if the next prompt is supplied by a process with latency --- for instance an external vision-based controller rather than the algorithmic orchestrator used here --- the policy holds its terminal pose rather than drifting while it waits. The tail also provides a natural completion signal: because a finished subtask manifests as the arm coming to rest, a higher-level controller can in principle infer that the policy considers the subtask done simply by detecting that it has stopped moving. The resulting SmolVLA model is therefore trained as a subtask executor rather than as a monotask policy: after reaching a subtask-terminal configuration, its learned behaviour should settle and wait rather than continue acting.

\subsection{Orchestrator Design}
\label{subsec:orchestrator}

The orchestrator is a finite-state controller wrapped around the VLA policy. The learned policy is only responsible for executing three manipulation prompts: GRASP(\textit{target}), LIFT, and PLACE. The controller chooses the next target, sends the corresponding prompt, monitors whether the expected physical outcome has occurred, and decides whether to advance, retry, redirect, or terminate the episode. Success checks use privileged simulator state so that the experiment evaluates the control structure rather than visual state estimation.

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{orchestrator_decision_flow.pdf}
    \caption{Decision logic of the orchestrated evaluator. The VLA is queried only for manipulation subtasks; target selection, local retries, target redirection, and the home-pose reset are handled by the controller.}
    \label{fig:orchestrator_flow}
\end{figure}

At the beginning of an episode, and after each completed placement, the controller enters \texttt{SELECT\_TARGET}. It reads the current orange positions, removes oranges already detected in the plate, assigns relative labels such as left, middle, and right, and selects a target for the next GRASP prompt. If no unplaced oranges remain, the episode is considered complete. If a previous GRASP timed out, the controller stores that orange as the last failed target and avoids selecting it when another unplaced orange is available. This target switch is counted as \textbf{target redirection}.

The three learned subtasks are guarded by explicit physical checks. GRASP is confirmed when the gripper is centred on an unplaced orange, the fingertips are closed, and projected contact force is detected on both gripper contacts for consecutive frames. If the physical check confirms a different orange from the requested one, the controller adapts and treats the actually grasped orange as the active target. LIFT is confirmed when the active orange remains held and rises at least \SI{0.06}{m} above its initial height. PLACE is confirmed when the active orange lies inside the oriented plate bounds, remains stable for consecutive frames, and is released with the gripper open.

Each manipulation state has a timeout: 700 steps for GRASP, 400 for LIFT, and 500 for PLACE. A confirmed GRASP advances to LIFT; a confirmed LIFT advances to PLACE; a confirmed PLACE either returns to \texttt{SELECT\_TARGET} for the next orange or ends the episode once all three oranges are in the plate.

\textbf{Recovery is defined at two levels of the goal hierarchy} --- the task (place all three oranges), the per-orange goal (GRASP\,$\rightarrow$\,LIFT\,$\rightarrow$\,PLACE for one orange), and the individual subtask. Pinning every recovery action to a level keeps the term precise and lets the same definition apply to both formulations. The orchestrator implements one recovery mechanism at each level, plus a precondition that enables the higher one. The three mechanisms must not be conflated.

\textbf{Local retry is Level-1 (sub-goal) recovery}: it recovers the \emph{same} orange. If the orange is dropped during LIFT or PLACE, or if LIFT/PLACE times out, the controller preserves the same target and returns directly to GRASP. The original sub-goal is still active, and no target-change reset is inserted.

\textbf{Target redirection is Level-2 (task) recovery}: it recovers task \emph{progress} by switching to a \emph{different} orange. After a GRASP timeout, when another unplaced orange is available, the controller intentionally selects a different orange so the episode keeps scoring instead of stalling on an unreachable target. The original sub-goal is dropped, but the task objective --- which is indifferent to \emph{which} orange is placed --- is served, so this is recovery at the task level rather than mere abandonment. If no alternative target remains, the controller retries the same orange (a Level-1 retry). Redirection is tracked as a mechanism counter; oranges are not permanently marked as abandoned in the final episode metrics.

\textbf{Home-pose reset is a precondition, not a recovery level.} Its purpose is to return the arm to the kind of \emph{start configuration} the policy was trained from. Teleop episodes begin from such a pose and then go on to grasp \emph{different} oranges depending on the instruction, so it is precisely from these states that the policy learned to ground the language prompt --- to choose a target from the text rather than to continue a motion already under way. VLA language conditioning is reliable only from such states, when the arm is far from all oranges; this spatial context-dependence is examined in \cref{sec:obedience}. Any Level-2 target change must therefore be preceded by this reset. When GRASP times out, the controller clears the active target, bypasses the VLA, and drives the arm back to the start pose along a short scripted trajectory: it first raises the gripper cleanly upward and only then returns to the starting joint configuration, so the arm disengages from the current orange without disturbing the others on the table. After the reset completes, the policy action queue is cleared and target selection resumes from a state where a new language target is reliable. A Level-1 retry on the same target after a slip needs no such reset.

\subsection{Subtask Outcomes and Failure Modes}
\label{subsec:failure_taxonomy}

Each subtask has a single intended physical outcome, confirmed by the privileged checks of \cref{subsec:orchestrator}. A failure is the negation of that check and falls into one of three physical modes. A \textbf{grasp failure} means the target orange is never acquired: the gripper never closes on it with confirmed contact. A \textbf{lift failure} means an acquired orange is never raised at least \SI{0.06}{m} above its start height, either because that height is never reached or because the orange slips from the gripper during the lift. A \textbf{place failure} means a lifted orange never comes to rest inside the plate bounds, either because it is never deposited or because it is dropped outside the plate during the release. Because each outcome is read directly from simulator state, these three modes are well defined for any rollout, independent of how the task is framed.

What differs between formulations is not the failure modes themselves but how they are detected and what follows. In the orchestrated subtask formulation each subtask carries a step budget --- 700 steps for GRASP, 400 for LIFT, and 500 for PLACE --- and a subtask is declared failed when its success check is not met within that budget. The timeout is therefore a \emph{detection trigger}, not a fourth failure mode: it is what lets the orchestrator notice the physical failure and respond with one of the mechanisms of \cref{subsec:orchestrator} --- a local retry after a slip, or a home-pose reset followed by target redirection after a GRASP timeout. GRASP is the one subtask that can only ever be declared failed by timeout, since no instantaneous physical event marks a grasp as impossible; LIFT and PLACE can additionally fail the moment the orange is lost.

These three failure modes map cleanly onto the two recovery levels of \cref{subsec:orchestrator}, and the mapping is what makes the recovery metrics in \cref{sec:experiments} fair across formulations. A \textbf{LIFT drop} or \textbf{PLACE slip} happens to an orange that was demonstrably held, so its identity is read directly from contact; ``the same orange'' is therefore unambiguous for both formulations, and these setbacks are the natural trigger for \textbf{Level-1} (same-orange) recovery. A \textbf{GRASP failure}, by contrast, never establishes contact, so no orange identity can be read from physics --- attributing the attempt to a specific intended orange would require guessing, which the monotask trace cannot do reliably. A grasp dead-end is exactly the case \textbf{Level-2} (different-orange) recovery addresses, and Level-2 needs no such attribution: it only asks whether a \emph{different} orange is subsequently placed. Measuring each failure at the level where the worked orange is unambiguous lets both formulations be scored by the same physical reads, with no inferred targets.

The monotask formulation has no such per-subtask structure. The policy runs end-to-end under a single global prompt within one 5000-step episode budget, with no intermediate checkpoints and therefore no per-subtask timeout. The same three physical failure modes still occur, but they are never detected or acted on during the episode: without per-subtask checkpoints, nothing verifies an outcome before the policy continues under the single global prompt. To compare the two formulations on a common footing, the during-episode analysis in \cref{sec:experiments} reconstructs the same physical events for monotask runs offline from privileged state --- inferring the worked target from the orange layout --- so that grasp, lift, and place outcomes can be read for both formulations even though only the orchestrated one observes them online.

\subsection{Autonomous Data Generation}

The autonomous data generation phase used the orchestrated execution system to collect training data without manual teleoperation. This phase was motivated by the first formal evaluation of the Teleop subtask model: subtasking plus orchestration did not improve full-task performance over the SmolVLA monotask baseline. The next hypothesis was therefore that the subtask policy needed more data, especially data drawn from the states it visits during autonomous execution rather than only from manual demonstrations.

A full autonomous rollout can contain both failed and successful subtasks. Rather than discarding an entire rollout when a subtask fails, the system records the successful subtask trajectories and discards the failed ones.

This produces an automatically generated dataset of successful subtasks collected under the current policy's own execution distribution. This report refers to the balanced retained subset as \textbf{Auto}. Target redirection plays an important role here: without the ability to pivot to a different orange after a GRASP timeout, the system would need to discard the entire rollout. Redirection allows the system to continue collecting successful subtask data from scenes that would otherwise be wasted.

\textbf{A reinforcement-learning reading.} This filter-and-retrain loop can be viewed as a rudimentary form of reinforcement learning. The task offers no dense reward to shape behaviour; instead, the orchestrator's privileged success checks provide a \emph{sparse, binary} one --- a subtask trajectory earns reward 1 if it reaches its intended physical outcome and 0 otherwise. Rolling the current policy out on freshly randomised scenes, keeping only the rewarded trajectories, and fine-tuning on them is then a single iteration of reward-filtered self-imitation: it is equivalent to reward-weighted behavioural cloning in which the weight is simply the success indicator. The scheme is deliberately minimal. Because the reward is a single bit for an entire subtask, it performs no temporal credit assignment within the trajectory: every transition of a retained subtask is treated as equally good, and no action is up- or down-weighted relative to the others as a true advantage estimate would be. It is thus the crudest form of policy improvement from self-generated experience --- keep the successes, discard the failures, and clone --- with no value function, no advantage, and a single update round. That simplicity is precisely why it was worth trying first: it reuses the existing orchestrator and imitation-learning pipeline unchanged, and only if this cheapest possible data loop helped would a more elaborate weighting be justified.

The autonomous generation run produced subtask episodes that were then filtered to balance the dataset by scene state: the number of episodes collected with one or two oranges already in the plate was equalised against those collected at the start of the episode, so the merged dataset does not over-represent early-scene behaviour. After filtering, 414 Auto episodes were retained and merged with the 846 Teleop training episodes to form \textbf{Teleop+Auto}, a 1260-episode subtask dataset. A new model --- referred to as the Teleop+Auto subtask model --- was trained on this combined dataset.

\subsection{Monotask Fair Comparison}

A subtask model differs from the LightwheelAI Baseline in both its training distribution and its task formulation, so a comparison against that baseline cannot attribute any difference to subtasking alone. To isolate the formulation, each subtask model is paired with a \textbf{monotask} counterpart trained on the \emph{same} trajectories. The per-subtask instructions are replaced by the single fixed prompt ``Place the orange into plate'', the terminal freeze tails are removed, and the model is run end-to-end without the orchestrator's GRASP/LIFT/PLACE checkpoints. The two members of a pair therefore see identical demonstrations and differ only in how the task is framed --- at both training and execution time. Every run, monotask or subtask, is evaluated under the same 5000-step episode budget.

The comparison is matched in data but not in temporal supervision, and this is intentional, since the supervision is part of the formulation under test. Through the freeze tails, the subtask model is taught to settle into a stable hold at each subtask boundary; the monotask model never sees these boundaries and is trained to keep acting under one global prompt. The results should accordingly be read as comparing two formulations, not as varying a single controlled factor.

A further caveat concerns what the training fragments represent: neither model is trained on a genuine end-to-end trajectory of a full three-orange episode. The monotask training data is itself reused subtask fragments sharing a single prompt rather than continuous runs, and the Teleop subtasks were curated for quality in isolation. We discuss the consequences of this coverage gap in \cref{sec:discussion}.
