\section{Results}
\label{sec:experiments}

This section compares task formulations and training sources. Recall the dataset names: \textbf{LightwheelAI Baseline} (60 public end-to-end demonstrations), \textbf{Teleop} (manually collected subtasks), \textbf{Auto} (balanced auto-generated successful subtasks), and \textbf{Teleop+Auto} (their merge).

Every completed run uses the same 100-scene reference seed set (\texttt{pick\_orange\_reference\_100\_v1}) in the same order, randomising the top-camera pose and the orange positions, so differences between models are attributable to policy and formulation rather than to easier or harder sampled scenes.

\subsection{Overall Performance}

Every model is evaluated on the same 100 seeded scenes with a 5000-step budget. Two numbers summarise the outcomes: the \emph{full-task success rate} (share of episodes that end with all three oranges in the plate) and the \emph{mean orange count} (average number placed, so partial progress counts). The figure below shows every model, one panel per fine-tuning recipe (\cref{subsec:finetuning}), the same model family in the same column throughout: \emph{standard} (top) trains only the action expert and state projection, the whole pretrained backbone frozen; \emph{LM-tuned} (middle, \emph{partial} in the figure) also trains the language model, vision encoder still frozen; \emph{fully-tuned} (bottom, \emph{full}) trains everything, vision encoder included. Each panel states its batch size and gradient steps. A fourth variant, \emph{no-tail} --- standard fine-tuning on data without the terminal freeze frames --- is reported inline only.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{orange_outcome_recipes.pdf}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{ACT vs.\ SmolVLA.} The LightwheelAI Baseline (top panel, first column) is where the two policy types are compared: ACT, a standard imitation-learning baseline, against SmolVLA, a state-of-the-art VLA in the small-model category. Run end-to-end under a single instruction, SmolVLA places all three oranges in 41\% of episodes against 13\% for ACT, averages 1.95 oranges against 0.92, and leaves only 9\% of episodes empty against 53\%. SmolVLA on this dataset is also the best full-task result under standard fine-tuning. Even so, 41\% is far from reliable, which is what the subtask decomposition aims to improve.

\paragraph{Decomposition helps partial progress more than full-task completion.} On the same trajectories, the subtask formulation's benefit under standard fine-tuning is mainly in partial progress. On Teleop it raises mean occupancy sharply (1.02 to 1.77) while raising full-task success only modestly (14\% to 20\%): checkpoints convert empty and one-orange episodes into two-orange ones, rarely into three. On Teleop+Auto the picture is mixed --- the monotask model is ahead on full-task success (18\% vs.\ 13\%) but behind on mean occupancy (1.29 vs.\ 1.42). Decomposition thus raises mean occupancy, but its effect on completion is small and inconsistent across sources --- only under full fine-tuning (below) does the subtask formulation pull ahead on both.

\paragraph{Subtasking rarely fails completely.} The standard models' clearest gap is on the weakest outcome --- an episode with no orange placed. The subtask models almost always place at least one (97\% of Teleop episodes, 84\% on Teleop+Auto), whereas the monotask models leave far more empty (only 56\% and 64\% reach one); \cref{sec:failure_modes} traces the gap to what happens after a grasp stall. For the fully-tuned models the gap nearly closes: their monotask variants almost never end empty either.

\paragraph{Autonomous data helped the monotask but hurt the subtask model --- under the standard recipe.} Adding the Auto episodes to Teleop improved the standard monotask model (full-task 14\% to 18\%, mean 1.02 to 1.29) yet regressed the subtask model on both metrics (20\% to 13\%, 1.77 to 1.42), even though the extra episodes were collected in the orchestrated setting the subtask model runs in; \cref{sec:discussion} returns to why.

\paragraph{Partial backbone tuning is inconsistent.} The middle panel trains the language model as well and the direction depends on source and formulation: full-task success rises on the Teleop subtask model (20\% to 33\%, mean 1.77 to 1.88) and the Teleop+Auto monotask model (18\% to 25\%, 1.29 to 1.54), but falls on the Teleop monotask model (14\% to 9\%, 1.02 to 0.92) and the Teleop+Auto subtask model (13\% to 10\%, 1.42 to 1.37). \cref{sec:obedience} shows the LM-tuned models do not follow their target instruction any better either. The \emph{no-tail} variant (not shown in the figure) --- a pure data change, keeping standard fine-tuning --- gives the Teleop subtask model a similar gain (20\% to 32\%).

\paragraph{Full fine-tuning is decisive.} The bottom panel unfreezes everything, vision encoder included, and improves \emph{every} family: 41\% to \textbf{61\%} on the LightwheelAI Baseline --- now the best result in the whole comparison --- 14\% to 54\%, 20\% to 58\%, 18\% to 36\%, and 13\% to 44\% on the four custom families. On the Teleop subtask model the mechanism is visible downstream: per-attempt grasp success rises from 37\% to 55\% (\cref{sec:failure_modes}). At this strength the merged data also hurts \emph{both} formulations (54 vs.\ 36, 58 vs.\ 44). For memory reasons every fully-tuned model trains under a different regime (batch 32, \num{40000} steps; \cref{subsec:limitations}); the \emph{regime control} in the middle panel --- the LM-tuned Teleop subtask model retrained under it, 33\% to 40\% --- bounds that contribution as minor. The LightwheelAI comparison carries no such caveat: its standard model already used batch 32 and \num{40000} steps, so its 20-point gain is unfreezing alone.

\subsection{Failure Analysis}

The overview above reports \emph{what} each model achieves; this section locates where execution breaks down and which step caps performance. The step-level analysis reads the \textbf{standard} models --- the design point every family shares; the tuning variants shift these magnitudes (grasping most) but not the structure, except where noted.

\subsubsection{Grasp Acquisition and Recovery Behaviour}
\label{sec:failure_modes}

\textbf{Grasp acquisition is the limiting step.} For the orchestrated (subtask) models --- the only ones whose grasp attempts are real, budgeted events rather than a heuristic reconstruction --- only about \textbf{37\%} of grasp attempts secure an orange under standard fine-tuning; the rest time out without acquiring one. The fully-tuned variant lifts this to 55\%. Everything downstream is reliable: lift and place each succeed 80--92\% of the time (higher still for the fully-tuned models), so once an orange is held it reaches the plate \textbf{87\%} of the time on Teleop. The cap on full-task success is upstream, at acquiring the orange at all.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{failure_modes.pdf}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Recovery is compared at matched stalls, on the standard models.} If \textbf{Level-2} recovery works, then whenever the policy is stuck on an orange it cannot grasp, the orchestrated formulation should escape --- switch to another orange and keep scoring --- where the monotask policy, which has no such mechanism, should stay stuck. Comparing the two requires knowing, on both sides, which orange the policy is working on and when that effort has failed. For the subtask both are explicit: the target is named in the GRASP prompt, and failure is the prompt timing out after its 700-step budget. For the monotask neither is observable --- no prompt names a target --- so its intent is approximated by \emph{proximity}: the orange it is working on is taken to be the nearest not-yet-placed orange to the gripper. A \textbf{stall} is then defined for both formulations as one grasp budget of consecutive commitment to a single orange without a force-confirmed grasp ($\geq$10\,N on both fingertips): for the subtask, exactly the first timed-out GRASP prompt on a target; for the monotask, 600 consecutive steps nearest to the same orange with nothing secured. The lower threshold compensates the proximity clock starting late (an orange only becomes nearest partway into an approach, roughly 130--150 steps in, judging by successful acquisitions), and no conclusion below changes for any threshold between 500 and 700. Two filters apply identically to both formulations: a stall counts only if another orange remains unplaced (so redirection is possible) and if at least 1500 of the 5000 episode steps remain (more than the median time to the next placement, so recovery is not cut short). The table then follows each stall: was the next orange worked on a \emph{different} one (\emph{redirected}), was any orange placed afterwards --- split by whether that first placement was the \emph{same} committed orange or a \emph{different} one --- and how many steps it took.

\textbf{After a stall, the orchestrated formulation recovers more often and sooner.} Reading \cref{tab:level2_deadline} column by column:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep, topsep=2pt, leftmargin=1.6em]
    \item \emph{Redirected} --- the two formulations first respond differently. The orchestrator redirects \emph{every} time: Level-2 recovery firing by design, spending at most one grasp budget on a failing orange before switching. The monotask policy is not incapable of switching --- it eventually moves away from the stalled orange in about half the cases here; why the other half stays is taken up in \cref{sec:discussion}.
    \item \emph{Placed an orange after} --- the central result. After the same fruitless effort on one orange, the orchestrated formulation goes on to place an orange far more often on Teleop (71\% of stalls against 31\%) and somewhat more often on Teleop+Auto (43\% against 34\%).
    \item \emph{Same vs.\ different} --- the gain is tied to redirection. Recovery through the \emph{same} orange is similar for both formulations in this table, 10--18\% of stalls (for the orchestrated formulation, which never persists past a stall, these are the minority of cases where a later redirection led \emph{back} to the abandoned orange); the entire gap sits in the \emph{different-orange} column, 53\% vs.\ 16\% on Teleop and 34\% vs.\ 17\% on Teleop+Auto. Re-grasping the \emph{same} orange after a slip --- \textbf{Level-1} recovery --- remains marginal (only 6--11\% of the standard models' placed oranges took more than one grasp).
    \item \emph{Median steps} --- the orchestrated formulation also tends to recover sooner (median 1030 vs.\ 1440 steps on Teleop), but the step counts carry less weight than the rates: the monotask stall rests on the approximate 600-step proximity threshold, so the timing lead is indicative rather than exact.
\end{itemize}
Only the redirection \emph{behaviour} itself is attributed to the recovery logic; the conversion rates are outcomes, to which the policy's per-attempt grasp quality also contributes.

\subsubsection{Grasp Obedience}
\label{sec:obedience}

Only the orchestrated runs name their target by spatial label (``Grasp \emph{<position>} orange''), so only they can be asked whether the policy grasps the orange it was \emph{told} to --- a grasp is \textbf{obeyed} when the gripper closes on the requested orange and a \textbf{misgrab} otherwise. The subtask policies obey only about \textbf{two-thirds to three-quarters} of the time (64--73\%), and \textbf{training more of the VLM does not help}: the LM-tuned variants obey 67\% on both sources and the fully-tuned variants 64\% on both --- the lowest of all, despite being the strongest models overall. Better grasping does not bring better instruction following.

\textbf{The misses are systematically leftward.} On the dominant left--right axis a missed request lands on the orange just to the \emph{left} of the one asked for (\emph{right} taken as \emph{middle}, \emph{middle} as \emph{left}), toward the side where the plate sits. So \emph{right} is the worst-followed label (27--47\%), while the leftmost and upper referents are almost always obeyed (82--100\%, one outlier at 71\%). It is genuine behaviour, not a logging artefact: the grasped orange is identified from contact, a physically distinct one a few centimetres to the side of the one requested.

\textbf{The two vertical labels blur into one another} --- and asymmetrically: a \emph{bottom right} request lands on the top-right orange in 29--62\% of cases, while \emph{top right} is almost never taken for \emph{bottom right} (0--10\%). These composite labels arise only when two oranges sit within 3\,cm laterally and must be told apart along the perpendicular axis --- and they are exactly the labels whose training supervision is scarcest and least reliably bounded (\cref{subsec:limitations}).
