\section{Results}
\label{sec:experiments}

This section compares task formulations and training sources. Recall the dataset names: \textbf{LightwheelAI Baseline} (60 public full-task demonstrations), \textbf{Teleop} (manually collected subtasks), \textbf{Auto} (balanced auto-generated successful subtasks), and \textbf{Teleop+Auto} (their merge).

Every completed run uses the same 100-scene reference seed set (\texttt{pick\_orange\_reference\_100\_v1}) in the same order; the randomised quantities are the top-camera pose and the orange positions. Differences between models are therefore attributable to policy and formulation rather than to easier or harder sampled scenes.

\paragraph{Decomposition helps partial progress more than full-task completion.} On the same trajectories, the subtask formulation's benefit is mainly in partial progress. On Teleop it raises mean occupancy sharply (1.02 to 1.77) while raising full-task success only modestly (14\% to 20\%): checkpoints convert empty and one-orange episodes into two-orange ones, rarely into three. On Teleop+Auto the picture is mixed --- the monotask model is ahead on full-task success (18\% vs.\ 13\%) but behind on mean occupancy (1.29 vs.\ 1.42). Decomposition thus raises mean occupancy on Teleop, but its effect on full-task completion is small and inconsistent across sources.

\paragraph{Subtasking rarely fails completely.} The clearest advantage is on the weakest outcome --- an episode with no orange placed. The subtask models almost always place at least one (97\% of Teleop episodes, 84\% on Teleop+Auto), whereas the monotask models leave far more empty (only 56\% and 64\% reach one). Target redirection is the likely reason --- but not because the monotask fixates on a single orange: it actually approaches about two distinct oranges per episode (\cref{sec:failure_modes}), yet, lacking a deliberate give-up-and-redirect, it often exhausts the budget without securing any, while the controller detects a repeated GRASP timeout and redirects to another reachable orange. The decomposed formulation thus rarely fails totally, even when it does not complete the task.

\paragraph{Aggregating autonomous data did not help.} Adding the Auto episodes to Teleop regressed the subtask model on both metrics (full-task 20\% to 13\%, mean 1.77 to 1.42). In-distribution successful subtasks were thus not sufficient to improve full-task behaviour; the subtask-level analysis below isolates what degraded.

\subsection{Failure Analysis}

The overview above reports \emph{what} each model achieves; this section digs into \emph{why} --- where execution breaks down and which step caps performance.

\subsubsection{Grasp Acquisition and Recovery Behaviour}
\label{sec:failure_modes}

\textbf{Grasp acquisition is the limiting step.} For the orchestrated (subtask) models --- the only ones whose grasp attempts are real, budgeted events rather than a heuristic reconstruction --- only about \textbf{37\%} of grasp attempts secure an orange; the rest time out without acquiring one. Everything downstream is reliable: lift and place each succeed 80--92\% of the time, so once an orange is held it reaches the plate \textbf{87\%} of the time on Teleop. The cap on full-task success is upstream, at acquiring the orange at all. (\emph{Secured} below is a force-confirmed grasp for the subtask models and, for the monotask ones --- which lift without force-gating --- a lift.)

\textbf{Local retry rarely decides a placement.} Re-grasping the \emph{same} orange after a slip --- the orchestrator's \textbf{Level-1} recovery --- is seldom what lands an orange: only 12 of the 186 secured-and-placed oranges took more than one grasp.

\textbf{The orchestrator tries more oranges than it secures.} It attempts about \textbf{2.7} distinct oranges per episode against the $\sim$2 it ever secures: \textbf{Level-2} redirection, giving up on a hard orange to try another. The end-to-end policy reaches a similar count only \emph{incidentally} --- about \textbf{2.0--2.2} distinct oranges per episode (monotask ``Oranges tried'' column above, recovered from per-step grip-axis proximity) --- so the gap is not that it never approaches a second orange, but that it does so without a deliberate give-up-and-switch and ends an episode empty far more often (36--44\% vs.\ 3--16\%).

\subsubsection{Target Obedience in the Subtask Policies}
\label{sec:obedience}

Only the orchestrated runs name their target by spatial label (``Grasp \emph{<position>} orange''), so only they can be asked whether the policy grasps the orange it was \emph{told} to. A grasp is \textbf{obeyed} when the gripper closes on the requested orange (matched by identity) and a \textbf{misgrab} otherwise. The policy follows the instruction only about \textbf{two-thirds} of the time on Teleop (67\%) and three-quarters on Teleop+Auto (73\%), per the confusion matrices below.

\textbf{The misses are systematically leftward.} They are not random: on the dominant left--right axis a missed request lands on the orange just to the \emph{left} of the one asked for --- \emph{right} taken as \emph{middle}, \emph{middle} as \emph{left} --- toward the side where the plate sits and the placed oranges accumulate. So \emph{right} is by far the worst-followed label (41\% on Teleop, 47\% on Teleop+Auto), while the leftmost and upper referents are almost always obeyed (96--97\%). It is genuine behaviour, not a logging artefact: the grasped orange is identified from contact, a physically distinct one a few centimetres to the side of the one requested.

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{grasp_obedience_confusion.pdf}
\end{figure}

\textbf{The weakest instruction is the one the task needs most.} Because the plate is on the left, each placement leaves the cleared oranges there and pushes the remaining targets rightward: the share of \emph{right} requests climbs from 19\% with all three oranges present, to 43\% after one placement, to 96\% after two. The policy is therefore least reliable in exactly the configuration the task keeps producing --- so changing target safely relies on the scripted spatial reset, not on the language instruction alone.
