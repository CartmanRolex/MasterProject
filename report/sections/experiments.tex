\section{Results}
\label{sec:experiments}

This section reports the experimental evidence collected so far. The emphasis is on factual comparisons between task formulations and training sources. The dataset names used throughout are: \textbf{LightwheelAI Baseline} for the 60-episode \texttt{leisaac-pick-orange-mimic-v0} demonstrations published by LightwheelAI, \textbf{Teleop} for the manually collected subtask dataset, \textbf{Auto} for the balanced automatically generated successful subtasks, and \textbf{Teleop+Auto} for their merged training set.

The current evaluations use the same reference seed set, \texttt{pick\_orange\_reference\_100\_v1}, for every completed model run. Each run therefore contains the same 100 initial conditions in the same order; across seed indices, the randomized quantities are the top-camera pose and the orange positions. This makes differences between bars more directly attributable to the policy and task formulation rather than to easier or harder sampled scenes.

\subsection{Full-Task Outcomes Across Formulations}

\Cref{fig:orange_outcome_distribution} reports final plate occupancy for every evaluated model on the shared 100-seed reference set, under an identical 5000-step episode budget. Two complementary metrics are read from it. The \emph{full-task success rate} is the fraction of episodes ending with all three oranges in the plate; it measures whether the long-horizon task is actually solved. The \emph{mean final occupancy} is the average number of oranges placed out of three; it credits partial progress and so separates models that make headway from those that stall early. Bars are grouped by training source --- LightwheelAI Baseline, Teleop, and Teleop+Auto --- and, within the two custom sources, by task formulation: a \emph{monotask} policy run end-to-end versus an orchestrated \emph{subtask} policy.

\paragraph{Subtasking improves over monotasking.} On the same training trajectories, the orchestrated subtask formulation outperforms the monotask one on both metrics. The effect is clearest on Teleop, where decomposition roughly doubles full-task success (13\% to 29\%) and lifts mean occupancy from 0.92 to 1.80, indicating that explicit subtask checkpoints convert otherwise-stalled episodes into progress. On Teleop+Auto the advantage narrows and splits between the two metrics: the subtask model retains a slight edge in mean occupancy (1.47 vs.\ 1.42) but a lower full-task success rate (13\% vs.\ 26\%). Decomposition therefore reliably helps a policy make partial progress, but on the merged data it does not translate that progress into complete-task success --- the controller secures more oranges on average yet less often finishes all three.

\paragraph{Subtasking rarely fails completely.} The gap is most striking on the weakest outcome --- ending an episode with no orange placed at all. The orchestrated subtask models almost always place at least one orange (89\% of episodes on Teleop, 84\% on Teleop+Auto), whereas the monotask models leave a large fraction of episodes empty (only 50\% reach one orange on Teleop, 69\% on Teleop+Auto). A plausible explanation lies in the orchestrator's target redirection: a monotask policy that fixates on a single hard-to-grasp orange can burn the entire episode without ever placing one, while the controller detects a repeated GRASP timeout, abandons that orange, and redirects to another. The decomposed formulation thus rarely produces a total failure, even when it does not complete the full task.

\paragraph{Aggregating autonomous data did not help.} Adding the automatically generated episodes to Teleop did not strengthen the policy. Under the orchestrated subtask formulation it in fact regressed performance on both metrics relative to Teleop alone (full-task success 29\% to 13\%, mean occupancy 1.80 to 1.47). Augmenting the dataset with in-distribution successful subtasks was thus not sufficient to improve full-task behaviour; the subtask-level analysis below isolates which behaviours degraded.

\subsection{Lift Failures and Recovery}
\label{sec:lift_recovery}

Beyond the final outcome, the per-step traces show how each policy reacts when a grasped orange slips during the lift. A \emph{lift} begins once a held orange rises above its initial height and \emph{drops} if it leaves the gripper before reaching the plate. \Cref{tab:lift_recovery} reports the per-attempt lift success rate and what follows a drop, for all four models. (The monotask traces are observer-inferred from physics, the subtask traces are the orchestrator's own log; the comparison should be read with that asymmetry in mind.)

The decisive difference is not the raw lift rate, which is comparable except for the weak Teleop monotask model, but the response to a drop. The subtask policies re-engage the dropped orange far more often than the monotask ones (83.9\% vs.\ 50.0\% on Teleop; 63.2\% vs.\ 25.0\% on Teleop+Auto) and so recover a much larger share of dropped oranges (72.7\% vs.\ 26.0\%; 38.5\% vs.\ 14.3\%). This is the local-retry mechanism made visible: the orchestrator returns to GRASP for the same orange, whereas the monotask policy drifts to a different one and rarely reclaims the dropped orange --- abandoning three of four on Teleop+Auto. When recovery does happen it is near-immediate (a single retry on average in every model), so the gap is driven by \emph{whether} the same orange is re-attempted, not how persistently.

\subsection{Target Obedience in the Subtask Policies}
\label{sec:obedience}

Only the orchestrated runs request a specific orange by spatial label (``Grasp \emph{<position>} orange''), so obedience --- did the policy grasp the orange it was told to --- is a subtask-only question. We first measure how often each positional referent appears in training, then how reliably it is obeyed at evaluation.

\paragraph{Instruction prevalence in the training data.} \Cref{tab:label_prevalence} counts the GRASP episodes carrying each positional label in the Teleop and Auto training sets, parsed from the language task strings. The distribution is heavily skewed: \emph{right} alone accounts for almost half of all grasp instructions (135/290 in Teleop, 209/433 combined), followed by \emph{middle} and \emph{left}, while the compound diagonal labels are rare. Adding the Auto episodes did not rebalance this --- it reinforced the same head of the distribution, contributing mostly more \emph{right} and \emph{middle} examples.

\paragraph{Obedience at evaluation.} A grasp is obedient when the grasped orange is the requested one \emph{by identity}, measured only over grasps that actually engaged an orange (timeouts are failures to grasp, not wrong-orange grasps). Globally, the subtask policies obey 75.1\% (193/257) of engaged grasps on Teleop and 77.0\% (187/243) on Teleop+Auto. This headline figure depends strongly on how many oranges remain, so \Cref{tab:obedience} breaks obedience down by the number already placed. We stop at one orange placed: with two placed only a single orange remains, which the labelling always calls ``right'' and which is trivially grasped (100\% in both models), so that case carries no information. The labels themselves are recomputed live but always over all three oranges including any already in the plate, which is why no ``left'' request survives once an orange is placed.

Two patterns stand out. First, obedience rises as the scene empties --- from 65--69\% with all three oranges present to 75--85\% with two, simply because fewer distractors make the referent easier to resolve. Second, the error is strongly directional: \emph{right} is the worst-obeyed label in every state, badly so when all three are present (24--32\%) and only partly recovered with two remaining (55--71\%), whereas \emph{left} and \emph{top right} are obeyed 88--97\%. Crucially, this ordering runs \emph{opposite} to training prevalence (\Cref{tab:label_prevalence}): the most heavily represented label, \emph{right}, is the worst obeyed, while the sparse \emph{top right} is obeyed best. Obedience is therefore not limited by how often a referent was seen --- the deficit cannot be closed simply by adding more examples of the failing labels, which is precisely what the Auto data did without effect. It points instead to a grounding problem: \emph{right} is a catch-all for the rightmost orange across many layouts and the region where the policy most often grasps a neighbour instead. This is direct evidence that the VLA's language conditioning is an imperfect, spatially biased channel rather than a clean selector --- the same context-dependence that motivates the scripted spatial reset.

\paragraph{Where the wrong grasps go.} The misgrabs are not random. \Cref{tab:misgrab} is a confusion matrix over the opening grasp of each episode: each row is a requested position and each column the position actually grasped (by identity), so the bold diagonal is the obeyed grasps and every off-diagonal entry is a specific substitution. A single directional bias dominates both models. When asked for \emph{right}, the policy overwhelmingly grasps the \emph{middle} orange instead (19 of 29 grasps on Teleop, 21 of 38 on Teleop+Auto); when asked for \emph{middle}, the errors fall on \emph{left} (10/28 and 9/35); and \emph{bottom right} is most often confused with its close-pair neighbour \emph{top right} (8/19 and 4/14). In other words the arm consistently grasps an orange to the \emph{left of, or inward from}, the one it was told to take, while the outermost referents --- \emph{left} and \emph{top right} --- are almost never confused. The deficit is thus concentrated geometrically, among targets that sit to the right of or between other oranges, exactly where a left-biased reach picks up the wrong neighbour.
