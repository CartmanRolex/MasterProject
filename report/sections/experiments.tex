\section{Results}
\label{sec:experiments}

This section reports the experimental evidence collected so far. The emphasis is on factual comparisons between task formulations: monolithic full-episode imitation, orchestrated subtask execution, additional autonomous subtask data, and a fair-comparison single-task model trained from the same handmade-plus-automatic data.

\subsection{Full-Episode Imitation Baseline}

The first experiment evaluates whether changing the policy class is sufficient when the complete pick-and-place task is treated as one monolithic episode. ACT and SmolVLA are trained on the same public HuggingFace dataset of 60 full-task demonstrations. In this setting, SmolVLA receives a single instruction for the whole episode, so the experiment does not evaluate meaningful language-conditioned control. Both policies are asked to solve the full task without subtask-level monitoring.

Figure~\ref{fig:baseline_comparison} shows the final plate occupancy distribution for both baselines. In all figures, \textit{N eval runs} denotes the number of evaluated episodes, and \textit{mean} denotes the average number of oranges in the plate out of three at the end of the episode.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.68\textwidth]{baseline_comparison.pdf}
    \caption{Final plate occupancy for ACT and SmolVLA trained on the public HuggingFace full-task dataset. Bar segments are percentages of evaluation episodes ending with 0, 1, 2, or 3 oranges in the plate.}
    \label{fig:baseline_comparison}
\end{figure}

The baseline distributions are comparable in aggregate. The ACT run has mean final occupancy 1.62/3, while the monolithic SmolVLA run has mean final occupancy 1.45/3. This supports the use of SmolVLA in later experiments because of its language-conditioning interface, not because it outperforms ACT in the monolithic setting.

\subsection{Full-Task Outcomes Across Formulations}

Figure~\ref{fig:orange_outcome_distribution} compares the main evaluated task formulations. The handmade subtask model is trained on 921 manually collected subtask episodes and executed through the orchestrator. The hand+auto subtask model is trained on those 921 episodes plus 414 successful autonomous subtask episodes. The final bar is a fair-comparison single-task SmolVLA model trained from the same handmade-plus-automatic data with the fixed prompt ``Place the orange into plate''.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{orange_outcome_distribution.pdf}
    \caption{Final plate occupancy across evaluated task formulations. Bar segments are percentages of evaluation episodes; \textit{N eval runs} is the number of evaluation episodes; \textit{mean} is average final oranges in plate out of three. Preliminary bars correspond to evaluations that were stopped before the planned 500 episodes.}
    \label{fig:orange_outcome_distribution}
\end{figure}

The handmade subtask model placed all three oranges in 26/326 evaluation runs (7.98\%) with mean final occupancy 1.32/3. It therefore did not improve full-task completion over the monolithic SmolVLA baseline. The current hand+auto subtask evaluation is preliminary: it has 1/41 full completions (2.44\%) and mean final occupancy 1.15/3. Adding automatically generated subtasks has not yet produced an observed full-completion improvement in the orchestrated subtask setting.

The single-task fair-comparison model has 43/247 full completions (17.41\%) and mean final occupancy 0.57/3. Its distribution is strongly all-or-nothing: most episodes end with 0 oranges, while a smaller set completes all three. On the same handmade-plus-automatic data, the orchestrated subtask model has lower full-task completion so far, but higher mean final occupancy. This indicates a different failure profile: the single-task model often gets stuck completely, whereas the orchestrated subtask model more often makes partial progress by moving on to other oranges after repeated failure on one target.

\subsection{Subtask-Level Outcomes}

Subtask-level metrics are available only for orchestrated evaluations, because the monolithic and single-task runs do not expose comparable GRASP, LIFT, and PLACE checkpoints. Table~\ref{tab:orchestrated_subtasks} reports success rates by subtask and by how many oranges were already in the plate at subtask start.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \small
    \caption{Subtask success rates during orchestrated evaluation, grouped by the number of oranges already in the plate at subtask start. The hand+auto rows are preliminary.}
    \label{tab:orchestrated_subtasks}
    \begin{tabular}{llccc}
        \toprule
        Model & Subtask & 0 placed & 1 placed & 2 placed \\
        \midrule
        Handmade & GRASP & 357/921 (38.8\%) & 204/608 (33.6\%) & 51/195 (26.2\%) \\
        Handmade & LIFT & 302/357 (84.6\%) & 174/204 (85.3\%) & 39/51 (76.5\%) \\
        Handmade & PLACE & 237/302 (78.5\%) & 140/174 (80.5\%) & 30/39 (76.9\%) \\
        \midrule
        Hand+auto & GRASP & 51/108 (47.2\%) & 17/107 (15.9\%) & 1/11 (9.1\%) \\
        Hand+auto & LIFT & 43/51 (84.3\%) & 13/17 (76.5\%) & 1/1 (100.0\%) \\
        Hand+auto & PLACE & 28/43 (65.1\%) & 9/13 (69.2\%) & 1/1 (100.0\%) \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

For the handmade model, GRASP is the lowest-success subtask and decreases as the episode progresses from 0 to 2 oranges already placed. LIFT and PLACE are higher once an orange has been acquired. In the preliminary hand+auto run, GRASP starts higher at 0 placed but drops sharply for later scene states; the 2-placed measurements are based on few attempts and should be treated cautiously.

The orchestrator mechanisms fired frequently in both orchestrated evaluations. In the handmade run, local retries occurred in 314/326 episodes and target redirections in 293/326 episodes, with 515 oranges abandoned in total. In the hand+auto preliminary run, local retries occurred in 40/41 episodes and target redirections in 40/41 episodes, with 76 oranges abandoned in total.
