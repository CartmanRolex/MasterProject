\section{Results}
\label{sec:experiments}

This section reports the experimental evidence collected so far. The emphasis is on factual comparisons between task formulations and training sources. The dataset names used throughout are: \textbf{LightwheelAI Baseline} for the 60-episode \texttt{leisaac-pick-orange-mimic-v0} demonstrations published by LightwheelAI, \textbf{Teleop} for the manually collected subtask dataset, \textbf{Auto} for the balanced automatically generated successful subtasks, and \textbf{Teleop+Auto} for their merged training set.

The current evaluations use the same reference seed set, \texttt{pick\_orange\_reference\_100\_v1}, for every completed model run. Each run therefore contains the same 100 initial conditions in the same order; across seed indices, the randomized quantities are the top-camera pose and the orange positions. This makes differences between bars more directly attributable to the policy and task formulation rather than to easier or harder sampled scenes.

\subsection{Full-Task Outcomes Across Formulations}

\Cref{fig:orange_outcome_distribution} reports final plate occupancy for every evaluated model on the shared 100-seed reference set, under an identical 5000-step episode budget. Two complementary metrics are read from it. The \emph{full-task success rate} is the fraction of episodes ending with all three oranges in the plate; it measures whether the long-horizon task is actually solved. The \emph{mean final occupancy} is the average number of oranges placed out of three; it credits partial progress and so separates models that make headway from those that stall early. Bars are grouped by training source --- LightwheelAI Baseline, Teleop, and Teleop+Auto --- and, within the two custom sources, by task formulation: a \emph{monotask} policy run end-to-end versus an orchestrated \emph{subtask} policy.

\paragraph{Decomposition helps partial progress, not full-task completion.} On the same training trajectories, the orchestrated subtask formulation's benefit is concentrated in partial progress rather than in finishing all three oranges. On Teleop it raises mean occupancy sharply (1.11 to 1.77) while leaving full-task success essentially flat (18\% to 20\%): explicit subtask checkpoints convert empty and one-orange episodes into two-orange ones, but rarely into all three. On Teleop+Auto the monotask model is in fact the stronger of the pair on both metrics (mean 1.50 vs.\ 1.42, full-task 26\% vs.\ 13\%). Decomposition therefore reliably secures more oranges on average on Teleop, but does not, on these runs, raise the rate of completing the whole task.

\paragraph{Subtasking rarely fails completely.} The clearest advantage is on the weakest outcome --- ending an episode with no orange placed at all. The orchestrated subtask models almost always place at least one orange (97\% of episodes on Teleop, 84\% on Teleop+Auto), whereas the monotask models leave far more episodes empty (only 57\% reach one orange on Teleop, 73\% on Teleop+Auto). A plausible explanation lies in the orchestrator's target redirection: a monotask policy that fixates on a single hard-to-grasp orange can burn the entire episode without ever placing one, while the controller detects a repeated GRASP timeout, abandons that orange, and redirects to another. The decomposed formulation thus rarely produces a total failure, even when it does not complete the full task.

\paragraph{Aggregating autonomous data did not help.} Adding the automatically generated episodes to Teleop did not strengthen the policy. Under the orchestrated subtask formulation it in fact regressed performance on both metrics relative to Teleop alone (full-task success 20\% to 13\%, mean occupancy 1.77 to 1.42). Augmenting the dataset with in-distribution successful subtasks was thus not sufficient to improve full-task behaviour; the subtask-level analysis below isolates which behaviours degraded.

\subsection{Lift Failures and Recovery}
\label{sec:lift_recovery}

Beyond the final outcome, the per-step traces show how each policy reacts when a grasped orange slips during the lift. A \textbf{lift} begins once a held orange rises above its initial height; it is a \textbf{drop} if the orange leaves the gripper before reaching the plate. From these traces we read three metrics, all reported in \Cref{tab:lift_recovery}:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=2pt,topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Lift success rate} --- successes as a fraction of genuine lift attempts (successes plus drops, excluding attempts cut short when the episode ends).
    \item \textbf{Drop-recovery rate} --- of the oranges whose \emph{first} lift dropped, the fraction nonetheless lifted later in the same episode (a placed orange counts as lifted).
    \item \textbf{Re-engagement} --- the fraction of drops whose \emph{next} lift attempt returns to the same orange rather than moving to a different one: the signature of a \emph{local retry}.
\end{itemize}

One caveat frames the comparison: the monotask traces are observer-inferred from physics, whereas the subtask traces are the orchestrator's own log.

\paragraph{The subtask policies lift more reliably.} Lift success is markedly higher under the subtask formulation (89--92\% vs.\ 61--83\% for monotask), most starkly on Teleop where the monotask model loses nearly two of every five held oranges. \textbf{The per-subtask checkpoints make the lift itself more reliable}, not merely recoverable.

\paragraph{Recovery after a drop is suggestive but small-sample here.} Because subtask drops are now rare, the post-drop columns of \Cref{tab:lift_recovery} rest on few events. On Teleop the subtask policy still re-engages the dropped orange far more often than the monotask one (77\% vs.\ 40\%), the signature of the orchestrator's \emph{local retry} returning to GRASP for the same orange --- a step the end-to-end monotask policy cannot take. On Teleop+Auto, with only a handful of subtask drops, re-engagement and recovery show no clear advantage. We therefore read drop-recovery and re-engagement as indicative rather than conclusive.

\subsection{Placing Failures}
\label{sec:place}

Placing the lifted orange into the plate is the final subtask. A \textbf{place} succeeds when the orange comes to rest in the plate, read directly from the recorded end-of-attempt geometry: the eval logs, for every attempt, whether the target orange is in the plate, independent of the gripper pose. The \textbf{place success rate} (\Cref{tab:place_success}) is the fraction of place attempts whose orange ends in the plate, split by \textbf{scene state} (oranges already placed when the place began). Because the success test is purely geometric, it applies uniformly to both formulations even though the monotask place attempts are observer-inferred.

\paragraph{The subtask policies place reliably; the Teleop monotask model struggles to start.} Once an orange has been lifted, the orchestrated subtask policies put it in the plate about four times in five (89\% Teleop, 82\% Teleop+Auto), roughly flat across scene state. The monotask models are more uneven: the \textbf{Teleop monotask succeeds on only 58\% of \emph{first} placements} but rises to 90--95\% once the plate is no longer empty --- the difficulty is getting the first orange in, not a late-episode collapse, mirroring the many empty episodes it leaves (\Cref{fig:orange_outcome_distribution}). On Teleop+Auto the two formulations place at a similar rate. \textbf{Placing is not where the subtask advantage comes from} --- once lifting has succeeded, both formulations finish the place comparably; the formulations diverge earlier, at getting an orange grasped and lifted at all.

\subsection{Target Obedience in the Subtask Policies}
\label{sec:obedience}

Only the orchestrated runs name their target by spatial label (``Grasp \emph{<position>} orange''), so we can ask whether the policy grasps the orange it was \emph{told} to --- a question the monotask runs cannot pose. A grasp is \textbf{obeyed} when the gripper closes on the requested orange, matched by orange identity rather than by position label, and counted only over grasps that actually engage an orange; any other engaged grasp is a \textbf{misgrab}. We report obedience by \textbf{scene state}: the number of oranges already in the plate when the grasp begins, since the difficulty of resolving a referent depends on how many distractors remain.

\paragraph{The instructions are dominated by one referent.} \Cref{tab:label_prevalence} counts the training GRASP episodes per label. \emph{right} alone accounts for almost half of them, with \emph{middle} and \emph{left} next and the diagonal labels rare; adding the Auto episodes only deepened this skew rather than broadening it. This sets up a clean test: \textbf{if obedience were limited by how often a referent was seen, the most common labels should be obeyed best.}

\paragraph{Obedience improves as the scene simplifies.} \Cref{fig:grasp_confusion} reports, for each model and scene state, how often each requested position was obeyed (green diagonal) and where the missed grasps landed (red, off-diagonal). Roughly three in four engaged grasps reach the requested orange overall, and the rate climbs steadily as oranges are cleared: it is lowest with all three present, higher once one has been placed, and trivially perfect with two placed (a single orange remains). \textbf{Fewer distractors make the spatial referent easier to resolve.}

\paragraph{Misgrabs follow a systematic inward bias.} The errors are far from random. In every panel of \Cref{fig:grasp_confusion} a single direction dominates: when a request is missed, the gripper closes on the orange immediately \textbf{to the left of, or inward from}, the intended one --- \emph{right} is taken as \emph{middle}, \emph{middle} as \emph{left}, and \emph{bottom right} as its close-pair \emph{top right}. The extreme referents \emph{left} and \emph{top right} are consequently obeyed far more reliably, while \emph{right} is the worst-obeyed label in both models and both scene states. We confirmed this reflects real behaviour, not a logging or labelling artefact: the grasped orange is identified from contact forces, the requested orange is verifiably the true rightmost, and the orange actually taken is a physically distinct one a few centimetres inward --- a genuine short reach in tightly packed scenes, not a mislabelling.

\paragraph{A grounding gap, not a coverage gap.} This obedience ordering runs \emph{opposite} to training prevalence: the most frequent label, \emph{right}, is the worst obeyed, and the sparse \emph{top right} among the best. \textbf{More examples of a referent do not buy better obedience} --- exactly what the Auto data shows, having added exposure without lifting it. The bottleneck is in \emph{grounding} the instruction to the current scene: the VLA's positional conditioning is a spatially biased channel rather than a clean target selector. This is the context-dependence that the scripted spatial reset is built to work around.
