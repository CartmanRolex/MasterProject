\section{Results}
\label{sec:experiments}

This section compares task formulations and training sources. Recall the dataset names: \textbf{LightwheelAI Baseline} (60 public full-task demonstrations), \textbf{Teleop} (manually collected subtasks), \textbf{Auto} (balanced auto-generated successful subtasks), and \textbf{Teleop+Auto} (their merge).

Every completed run uses the same 100-scene reference seed set (\texttt{pick\_orange\_reference\_100\_v1}) in the same order; the randomised quantities are the top-camera pose and the orange positions. Differences between models are therefore attributable to policy and formulation rather than to easier or harder sampled scenes.

\subsection{Full-Task Outcomes Across Formulations}

\Cref{fig:orange_outcome_distribution} reports final plate occupancy for every model on the shared 100-seed set under a 5000-step budget, via two metrics: the \emph{full-task success rate} (fraction of episodes ending with all three oranges placed --- whether the task is solved) and the \emph{mean final occupancy} (average oranges placed out of three --- crediting partial progress, separating headway from early stalls). Bars are grouped by training source and, within the two custom sources, by formulation (\emph{monotask} end-to-end vs.\ orchestrated \emph{subtask}).

\paragraph{Decomposition helps partial progress, not full-task completion.} On the same trajectories, the subtask formulation's benefit is in partial progress, not finishing all three. On Teleop it raises mean occupancy sharply (1.11 to 1.77) while leaving full-task success flat (18\% to 20\%): checkpoints convert empty and one-orange episodes into two-orange ones, rarely into three. On Teleop+Auto the monotask model is in fact stronger on both metrics (mean 1.50 vs.\ 1.42, full-task 26\% vs.\ 13\%). Decomposition thus secures more oranges on average on Teleop but does not raise full-task completion.

\paragraph{Subtasking rarely fails completely.} The clearest advantage is on the weakest outcome --- an episode with no orange placed. The subtask models almost always place at least one (97\% of Teleop episodes, 84\% on Teleop+Auto), whereas the monotask models leave many empty (only 57\% and 73\% reach one). Target redirection is the likely reason: a monotask policy can fixate on one hard-to-grasp orange and burn the episode, while the controller detects the repeated GRASP timeout and redirects to another. The decomposed formulation thus rarely fails totally, even when it does not complete the task.

\paragraph{Aggregating autonomous data did not help.} Adding the Auto episodes to Teleop regressed the subtask model on both metrics (full-task 20\% to 13\%, mean 1.77 to 1.42). In-distribution successful subtasks were thus not sufficient to improve full-task behaviour; the subtask-level analysis below isolates what degraded.

\subsection{Lift Failures and Recovery}
\label{sec:lift_recovery}

Beyond the final outcome, the per-step traces show how each policy reacts when a held orange slips during the lift. A \textbf{lift} begins once a held orange rises above its start height; it is a \textbf{drop} if the orange leaves the gripper before the plate. We read three metrics (\Cref{tab:lift_recovery}): lift reliability, and two measuring \textbf{Level-1 (same-orange) recovery} --- whether the dropped orange is itself reclaimed --- complemented by the formulation-fair task-level view in \cref{sec:task_recovery}:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=2pt,topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Lift success rate} --- successes / genuine lift attempts (successes + drops, excluding episode-end truncations).
    \item \textbf{Drop-recovery rate} --- of oranges whose \emph{first} lift dropped, the fraction lifted again later in the episode (a placed orange counts as lifted).
    \item \textbf{Re-engagement} --- the fraction of drops whose \emph{next} lift returns to the same orange: the signature of a \emph{local retry}. We report it for context, not as a fair head-to-head, since the monotask has no fixed target to return to.
\end{itemize}

One caveat frames every subtask-level comparison below: the monotask traces are reconstructed offline from physics (\cref{subsec:failure_taxonomy}), whereas the subtask traces are the orchestrator's own log.

\paragraph{The subtask policies lift more reliably.} Lift success is markedly higher under the subtask formulation (89--92\% vs.\ 61--83\% for monotask), most starkly on Teleop where the monotask model loses nearly two of every five held oranges. Part of this gap is by construction: the orchestrator enters LIFT only once its grasp check passes, so a subtask lift starts from a \emph{verified} solid grasp, whereas the monotask trace infers a lift from any upward motion of a held orange and counts lifts that begin from marginal grasps. The higher subtask rate is thus partly a better-conditioned starting point, not only a better policy.

\paragraph{Recovery after a drop is suggestive but small-sample.} Because subtask drops are now rare, the post-drop columns of \Cref{tab:lift_recovery} rest on few events. On Teleop the subtask policy re-engages the dropped orange more often than the monotask one (77\% vs.\ 40\%) --- the \emph{local retry} returns to GRASP by design, whereas the monotask reaches it only emergently, though still after two of every five drops. On Teleop+Auto, with only a handful of subtask drops, neither metric shows a clear advantage. We read these Level-1 columns as indicative, and turn to task-level recovery (\cref{sec:task_recovery}) for the comparison that does not hinge on same-orange behaviour.

\subsection{Placing Failures}
\label{sec:place}

The \textbf{place success rate} (\Cref{tab:place_success}) is the fraction of place attempts whose orange ends in the plate, split by \textbf{scene state} (oranges already placed when the place began). Being purely geometric, the test applies uniformly to both formulations.

\paragraph{The subtask policies place reliably; the Teleop monotask struggles to start.} Once an orange is lifted, the subtask policies place it about four times in five (89\% Teleop, 82\% Teleop+Auto), roughly flat across scene state. The monotask models are uneven: the \textbf{Teleop monotask succeeds on only 58\% of \emph{first} placements} but rises to 90--95\% once the plate is non-empty --- the difficulty is getting the first orange in, not a late collapse, mirroring its many empty episodes (\Cref{fig:orange_outcome_distribution}). On Teleop+Auto the two place similarly. \textbf{Placing is not the source of the subtask advantage}: once lifting succeeds both finish comparably; they diverge earlier, at grasping and lifting an orange at all.

\paragraph{A failed place rarely recovers.} Asking the same \textbf{Level-1} question as for the lift --- of oranges whose \emph{first} place failed, how often that orange is later placed (\Cref{tab:place_recovery}) --- the answer is seldom: 17--21\% under the subtask formulation and just 3\% for the Teleop monotask, well below the lift drop-recovery rates, with re-engagement only a quarter to a third of the time. \textbf{A slip at the plate is largely terminal}: unlike a dropped lift, which the local retry often reclaims, a failed place usually loses that orange for the episode. The subtask policy recovers somewhat more often, consistent with local retry, but the effect is small. As with the lift, this same-orange view favours the orchestrator by construction; \cref{sec:task_recovery} gives the formulation-fair picture.

\subsection{Task-Level Recovery}
\label{sec:task_recovery}

The lift and place analyses measure \textbf{Level-1 (same-orange) recovery}, the orchestrator's local retry --- which the monotask, never given a fixed target, has no mechanism to aim for. For an equal-footing comparison we also measure \textbf{Level-2 (task) recovery} (\cref{subsec:orchestrator}): after a setback, does the system keep scoring by placing \emph{some} orange, regardless of which? Because the objective is indifferent to which orange is placed, this is where monotask and subtask are genuinely comparable, needing no inference of intended target.

The trigger is the same setback for both formulations --- a LIFT drop or PLACE slip from simulator state --- and recovery is a later successful placement. \Cref{tab:task_recovery} reports two views. \emph{Score recovery}: of episodes with at least one setback, the fraction whose placed count rose afterward (same or different orange). \emph{Different-orange recovery}, the stricter Level-2 signal: of setbacks with at least one \emph{other} orange still unplaced, the fraction followed by placing a \emph{different} orange. The 5000-step budget is shared, so exhaustion is symmetric across formulations.

Denominators differ because the subtask policy suffers far fewer setbacks.}

\paragraph{Level-2 is where the orchestrator's advantage is real --- and where Auto data erodes it.} On Teleop the subtask formulation suffers far fewer setbacks (34 vs.\ 76 episodes) and recovers from them far more often: it keeps scoring in 73.5\% of episodes against 35.5\% for monotask, and reaches a \emph{different} orange after 60.0\% of setbacks against 23.1\%. This is the mechanism behind ``rarely fails completely'' (\cref{fig:orange_outcome_distribution}): Level-2 redirection turns dead-ends into progress the end-to-end policy, prone to fixating, does not make. The pattern reverses on Teleop+Auto, where the subtask model recovers \emph{less} often on both views (42.9\% vs.\ 53.7\%; 28.0\% vs.\ 35.1\%) --- the full-task regression now visible at the recovery level: the added data weakened exactly the task-level robustness the orchestrator exists to provide.

\subsection{Target Obedience in the Subtask Policies}
\label{sec:obedience}

Only the orchestrated runs name their target by spatial label (``Grasp \emph{<position>} orange''), so we can ask whether the policy grasps the orange it was \emph{told} to --- a question monotask runs cannot pose. A grasp is \textbf{obeyed} when the gripper closes on the requested orange (matched by identity, not position label), counted over grasps that engage an orange; any other engaged grasp is a \textbf{misgrab}. We report obedience by \textbf{scene state} (oranges already placed at grasp time), since resolving a referent depends on how many distractors remain.

\paragraph{The instructions are dominated by one referent.} \Cref{tab:label_prevalence} counts training GRASP episodes per label: \emph{right} alone is almost half, with \emph{middle} and \emph{left} next and diagonal labels rare; the Auto episodes only deepened this skew. This sets up a clean test: \textbf{if obedience were limited by exposure, the most common labels should be obeyed best.}

\paragraph{Obedience improves as the scene simplifies.} \Cref{fig:grasp_confusion} reports, per model and scene state, how often each requested position was obeyed (green diagonal) and where misses landed (red). Roughly three in four engaged grasps reach the requested orange, and the rate climbs as oranges clear: lowest with all three present, higher once one is placed, trivially perfect with two placed. \textbf{Fewer distractors make the referent easier to resolve.}

\paragraph{Misgrabs follow a systematic inward bias.} The errors are far from random: in every panel of \Cref{fig:grasp_confusion} a missed request closes on the orange immediately \textbf{inward of} the intended one --- \emph{right} taken as \emph{middle}, \emph{middle} as \emph{left}, \emph{bottom right} as \emph{top right}. The extreme referents \emph{left} and \emph{top right} are thus obeyed most reliably, while \emph{right} is the worst-obeyed label in both models and scene states. This is real behaviour, not a logging artefact: the grasped orange is identified from contact, the requested one is verifiably the true rightmost, and the orange taken is a physically distinct one a few centimetres inward --- a genuine short reach in packed scenes.

\paragraph{A grounding gap, not a coverage gap.} This ordering runs \emph{opposite} to training prevalence: the most frequent label \emph{right} is the worst obeyed, the sparse \emph{top right} among the best. \textbf{More examples do not buy better obedience} --- exactly what the Auto data shows, adding exposure without lifting it. The bottleneck is \emph{grounding} the instruction to the scene: the VLA's positional conditioning is a spatially biased channel, not a clean target selector --- the context-dependence the scripted spatial reset works around.
