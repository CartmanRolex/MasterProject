\section{Results}
\label{sec:experiments}

This section reports the experimental evidence collected so far. The emphasis is on factual comparisons between task formulations and training sources. The dataset names used throughout are: \textbf{LightwheelAI Baseline} for the 60-episode \texttt{leisaac-pick-orange-mimic-v0} demonstrations published by LightwheelAI, \textbf{Teleop} for the manually collected subtask dataset, \textbf{Auto} for the balanced automatically generated successful subtasks, and \textbf{Teleop+Auto} for their merged training set.

The current evaluations use the same reference seed set, \texttt{pick\_orange\_reference\_100\_v1}, for every completed model run. Each run therefore contains the same 100 initial conditions in the same order; across seed indices, the randomized quantities are the top-camera pose and the orange positions. This makes differences between bars more directly attributable to the policy and task formulation rather than to easier or harder sampled scenes.

\subsection{Full-Task Outcomes Across Formulations}

\Cref{fig:orange_outcome_distribution} reports final plate occupancy for every evaluated model on the shared 100-seed reference set, under an identical 5000-step episode budget. Two complementary metrics are read from it. The \emph{full-task success rate} is the fraction of episodes ending with all three oranges in the plate; it measures whether the long-horizon task is actually solved. The \emph{mean final occupancy} is the average number of oranges placed out of three; it credits partial progress and so separates models that make headway from those that stall early. Bars are grouped by training source --- LightwheelAI Baseline, Teleop, and Teleop+Auto --- and, within the two custom sources, by task formulation: a \emph{monotask} policy run end-to-end versus an orchestrated \emph{subtask} policy.

\paragraph{Subtasking improves over monotasking.} On the same training trajectories, the orchestrated subtask formulation outperforms the monotask one on both metrics. The effect is clearest on Teleop, where decomposition roughly doubles full-task success (13\% to 29\%) and lifts mean occupancy from 0.92 to 1.80, indicating that explicit subtask checkpoints convert otherwise-stalled episodes into progress. On Teleop+Auto the advantage narrows and splits between the two metrics: the subtask model retains a slight edge in mean occupancy (1.47 vs.\ 1.42) but a lower full-task success rate (13\% vs.\ 26\%). Decomposition therefore reliably helps a policy make partial progress, but on the merged data it does not translate that progress into complete-task success --- the controller secures more oranges on average yet less often finishes all three.

\paragraph{Subtasking rarely fails completely.} The gap is most striking on the weakest outcome --- ending an episode with no orange placed at all. The orchestrated subtask models almost always place at least one orange (89\% of episodes on Teleop, 84\% on Teleop+Auto), whereas the monotask models leave a large fraction of episodes empty (only 50\% reach one orange on Teleop, 69\% on Teleop+Auto). A plausible explanation lies in the orchestrator's target redirection: a monotask policy that fixates on a single hard-to-grasp orange can burn the entire episode without ever placing one, while the controller detects a repeated GRASP timeout, abandons that orange, and redirects to another. The decomposed formulation thus rarely produces a total failure, even when it does not complete the full task.

\paragraph{Aggregating autonomous data did not help.} Adding the automatically generated episodes to Teleop did not strengthen the policy. Under the orchestrated subtask formulation it in fact regressed performance on both metrics relative to Teleop alone (full-task success 29\% to 13\%, mean occupancy 1.80 to 1.47). Augmenting the dataset with in-distribution successful subtasks was thus not sufficient to improve full-task behaviour; the subtask-level analysis below isolates which behaviours degraded.

\subsection{Lift Failures and Recovery}
\label{sec:lift_recovery}

Beyond the final outcome, the per-step traces show how each policy reacts when a grasped orange slips during the lift. A \textbf{lift} begins once a held orange rises above its initial height; it is a \textbf{drop} if the orange leaves the gripper before reaching the plate. From these traces we read three metrics, all reported in \Cref{tab:lift_recovery}:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=2pt,topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Lift success rate} --- successes as a fraction of genuine lift attempts (successes plus drops, excluding attempts cut short when the episode ends).
    \item \textbf{Drop-recovery rate} --- of the oranges whose \emph{first} lift dropped, the fraction nonetheless lifted later in the same episode (a placed orange counts as lifted).
    \item \textbf{Re-engagement} --- the fraction of drops whose \emph{next} lift attempt returns to the same orange rather than moving to a different one: the signature of a \emph{local retry}.
\end{itemize}

One caveat frames the comparison: the monotask traces are observer-inferred from physics, whereas the subtask traces are the orchestrator's own log.

\paragraph{Lift success is comparable; the response to a drop is not.} Raw lift success is similar across formulations --- only the Teleop monotask model is markedly weaker --- so the decisive difference lies in what happens \emph{after} a drop. The subtask policies re-engage the dropped orange far more often than the monotask ones, and consequently recover a much larger share of dropped oranges; the monotask policies instead drift to a different orange and rarely reclaim the dropped one, abandoning the large majority of them on Teleop+Auto. \textbf{Decomposition does not make the lift itself more reliable --- it makes a failed lift recoverable.}

\paragraph{This is the local-retry mechanism made visible.} The gap is exactly what the orchestrator's local retry is built to produce: on a slip it returns to GRASP for the \emph{same} orange, a step the end-to-end monotask policy has no means to take. And when recovery does happen it is near-immediate --- a single retry suffices on average --- so \textbf{the gap is driven by \emph{whether} the dropped orange is re-attempted at all, not by how persistently it is retried.}

\subsection{Target Obedience in the Subtask Policies}
\label{sec:obedience}

Only the orchestrated runs name their target by spatial label (``Grasp \emph{<position>} orange''), so we can ask whether the policy grasps the orange it was \emph{told} to --- a question the monotask runs cannot pose. A grasp is \textbf{obeyed} when the gripper closes on the requested orange, matched by orange identity rather than by position label, and counted only over grasps that actually engage an orange; any other engaged grasp is a \textbf{misgrab}. We report obedience by \textbf{scene state}: the number of oranges already in the plate when the grasp begins, since the difficulty of resolving a referent depends on how many distractors remain.

\paragraph{The instructions are dominated by one referent.} \Cref{tab:label_prevalence} counts the training GRASP episodes per label. \emph{right} alone accounts for almost half of them, with \emph{middle} and \emph{left} next and the diagonal labels rare; adding the Auto episodes only deepened this skew rather than broadening it. This sets up a clean test: \textbf{if obedience were limited by how often a referent was seen, the most common labels should be obeyed best.}

\paragraph{Obedience improves as the scene simplifies.} \Cref{fig:grasp_confusion} reports, for each model and scene state, how often each requested position was obeyed (green diagonal) and where the missed grasps landed (red, off-diagonal). Roughly three in four engaged grasps reach the requested orange overall, and the rate climbs steadily as oranges are cleared: it is lowest with all three present, higher once one has been placed, and trivially perfect with two placed (a single orange remains). \textbf{Fewer distractors make the spatial referent easier to resolve.}

\paragraph{Misgrabs follow a systematic inward bias.} The errors are far from random. In every panel of \Cref{fig:grasp_confusion} a single direction dominates: when a request is missed, the gripper closes on the orange immediately \textbf{to the left of, or inward from}, the intended one --- \emph{right} is taken as \emph{middle}, \emph{middle} as \emph{left}, and \emph{bottom right} as its close-pair \emph{top right}. The extreme referents \emph{left} and \emph{top right} are consequently obeyed almost perfectly, while \emph{right} is the worst-obeyed label in both models and both scene states. We confirmed this reflects real behaviour, not a logging or labelling artefact: the grasped orange is identified from contact forces, the requested orange is verifiably the true rightmost, and the orange actually taken is a physically distinct one a few centimetres inward --- a genuine short reach in tightly packed scenes, not a mislabelling.

\paragraph{A grounding gap, not a coverage gap.} This obedience ordering runs \emph{opposite} to training prevalence: the most frequent label, \emph{right}, is the worst obeyed, and the sparse \emph{top right} among the best. \textbf{More examples of a referent do not buy better obedience} --- exactly what the Auto data shows, having added exposure without lifting it. The bottleneck is in \emph{grounding} the instruction to the current scene: the VLA's positional conditioning is a spatially biased channel rather than a clean target selector. This is the context-dependence that the scripted spatial reset is built to work around.
