\section{Discussion \& Conclusion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{The limitation is structural, not just the policy class.} Even the stronger SmolVLA monotask baseline solves only 41/100 episodes (\cref{fig:orange_outcome_distribution}), so changing the policy backbone alone does not make the long-horizon task reliable. The monotask formulation gives the policy no point at which an intermediate outcome is verified: a local failure simply continues under the same global prompt, plausibly leaving the scene in a rarely-seen state from which behaviour degrades --- a hypothesis (\cref{sec:introduction}), not a demonstrated mechanism.

\textbf{Decomposition creates real intervention points, but their payoff is narrow.} What the subtask checkpoints buy is the ability to give up on a hard orange and try another: on Teleop the orchestrated model attempts 2.7 distinct oranges per episode and ends empty only 3\% of the time, against 43\% for the end-to-end policy (\cref{sec:formulation}). The sliced-baseline experiment showed checkpoints alone are not enough, though: this redirection also needs the ability to request a specific orange --- which a fixed-order dataset cannot teach and the Teleop dataset was built to provide.

\textbf{Neither level substitutes for a reliable subtask policy.} The Teleop subtask model reaches 20/100 full-task success (mean 1.77/3) --- above its monotask pair (18/100, 1.11) but still below the 41/100 baseline. The bottleneck is GRASP: obedience is only about three in four and degrades as the scene fills (\cref{sec:obedience}), and explicit monitoring cannot place an orange the policy never grasps.

\textbf{Autonomous data did not fix this, and the analysis says why.} Adding 414 Auto subtasks \emph{reduced} orchestrated performance (13/100, mean 1.42); on the merged data the monotask is in fact stronger on both full-task metrics (26/100, 1.50), and the subtask's redirection edge shrinks accordingly (\cref{sec:formulation}). The obedience analysis explains it: the failure is a \emph{grounding} gap, not a coverage gap, since more examples of a referent did not improve obedience (\cref{sec:obedience}). More in-distribution successful data was therefore the wrong lever.

\textbf{Limitations.} Success checks use privileged simulator state, which deployment would have to replace with perception. Each completed evaluation is 100 seeded episodes, so the exact percentages are limited-sample estimates. The Auto set contains only successful subtasks from a weak policy, so it may reinforce a narrow visited-state distribution rather than broaden it. The per-subtask step budgets that declare a subtask timed-out are also somewhat arbitrary, and they interact with the tail-freeze training (\cref{subsec:teleop}): a subtask policy taught to idle once an orange is held or placed sometimes exhausts the budget while the orange is already in the plate. Place success is read from the orange's final position rather than the orchestrator's confirmation, so these cases count as successes, but they inflate the apparent per-subtask timeout counts (most visibly the Teleop+Auto subtask) and argue for retraining on a \emph{tail-free} dataset, where completion is signalled by the policy coming to rest rather than by a fixed budget. The monotask grasp trace is also reconstructed heuristically (its target is re-inferred every few frames), so we deliberately report no monotask grasp rate or attempt count, and cannot yet test whether the end-to-end policy \emph{fixates} on a single orange --- that needs per-step gripper--orange proximity logging, the first planned upgrade to the evaluation. Finally, neither model is trained on a complete episode (\cref{subsec:failure_taxonomy}): the ``full-task'' monotask data is relabelled subtask fragments and the Teleop subtasks were curated in isolation, so the data contains no full successful run, nor the imperfect mid-episode states a policy actually visits --- a coverage gap that plausibly limits both long-horizon chaining and off-distribution robustness.

\textbf{Conclusion.} This project frames recovery as an execution-level requirement for VLA-based manipulation and separates it into two levels --- sub-goal recovery by local retry and task recovery by redirection. Decomposition demonstrably creates the intervention points --- a confirmed grasp converts to a placement far more reliably on Teleop --- but the implemented system does not yet deliver full-task robustness: the mechanisms cannot compensate for an insufficiently reliable subtask policy, and adding autonomous data hurt rather than helped. The most useful next step is therefore not more autonomous data but better subtask-policy quality and coverage --- especially GRASP grounding and late-scene PLACE --- and collecting some complete, imperfect full-task trajectories to close the coverage gap, before expecting orchestration to raise full-task success.
