% ===== 2. The problem =====
\begin{frame}{Long-horizon manipulation: failure is the hard part}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item A pick-and-place task succeeds only if the robot \hl{grasps $\rightarrow$ lifts $\rightarrow$ places} --- in that order.
    \item The hard problem is not a single failed step, but \hl{what the system does \emph{after} a failure}.
    \item \textbf{Stagnation:} a policy can keep retrying one orange until the timeout, ignoring the others.
    \item \textbf{State poisoning (hypothesis):} a fumbled grasp can knock an orange into a rarely-seen state, from which the policy acts unreliably.
  \end{itemize}
  \vfill
  \begin{block}{The angle}
    Improve robustness by \hl{fixing the structure around the policy}, not just the policy itself.
  \end{block}
\end{frame}

% ===== 3. Why learned policies are brittle =====
\begin{frame}{Why learned policies are brittle here}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Trained on \hl{successful demonstrations} --- the training distribution is mostly ``things went well so far''.
    \item During autonomous execution, small deviations $\rightarrow$ \hl{out-of-distribution states} $\rightarrow$ errors accumulate over the long horizon.
    \item Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models add language as an interface --- e.g.\ \emph{``place the object in the plate''}.
    \item But that instruction \hl{assumes the object is already held}; language alone offers no way to \emph{detect} or \emph{interrupt} a failure.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

% ===== 4. Hypothesis & contributions =====
\begin{frame}{Idea: decompose, then orchestrate}
  \textbf{Hypothesis.} Long-horizon tasks become more robust if decomposed into \hl{language-conditioned subtasks} surrounded by an \hl{orchestrator} that checks each outcome and recovers from failure.
  \vfill
  \textbf{Contributions.}
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item A monotask baseline (\hl{ACT vs.\ SmolVLA}) --- changing the policy class alone is not enough.
    \item A targeted \hl{Teleop subtask dataset} + an orchestrator that monitors outcomes and performs \hl{retry / reset / redirect}.
    \item Using the orchestrator for \hl{autonomous data generation} --- and asking whether more data helps (the answer is \emph{not clear-cut}).
  \end{enumerate}
\end{frame}

% ===== 6. SmolVLA & fine-tuning variants =====
\begin{frame}{The policy: SmolVLA, and three fine-tuning variants}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Pretrained vision-language backbone (\texttt{SmolVLM2-500M}) + a small \hl{action expert} (flow matching).
    \item Outputs a \hl{chunk of 20 joint actions} per query (matches the 20-frame terminal hold used in data collection).
  \end{itemize}
  \vspace{0.3em}
  Every model is trained under one of three variants, compared in the results:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \hl{Standard} --- backbone frozen; train only the \hl{action expert + state projection} ($\approx$100M of 450M params).
    \item \hl{Partial} --- additionally trains the \hl{language model} ($\approx$316M); vision encoder still frozen.
    \item \hl{Full} --- trains \hl{everything}, vision encoder included (450M). Needs batch 32 / 40k steps for GPU memory; a matched control bounds what that regime change alone contributes.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{A custom subtask dataset: \emph{Teleop}}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \begin{column}{0.30\textwidth}
      \centering
      \vspace{0.6em}
      {\color{epflred}\fontsize{42}{46}\selectfont\textbf{846}}\\[0.3em]

    \end{column}
    \begin{column}{0.70\textwidth}
      \begin{itemize}
        \item Built from scratch: \hl{Xbox gamepad} $\rightarrow$ SO-101 in Isaac.
        \item 1 episode = \hl{1 successful subtask}, labelled with its instruction.
        \item \hl{Randomised pick order} $\rightarrow$ forces grounding of the position instruction (target $\neq$ task progress).
        \item \hl{``Freeze tail''} (20 frames) $\rightarrow$ the policy learns to \emph{wait} at a boundary instead of drifting.
        \item Trained SmolVLA as a \hl{subtask executor} (GRASP / LIFT / PLACE).
      \end{itemize}
    \end{column}
  \end{columns}

% ===== 10. Key insight =====
\begin{frame}{Key insight: language is spatially context-dependent}
  \begin{block}{The behavioural finding that motivates the orchestrator's reset}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Gripper near orange A $\Rightarrow$ ``grasp the \emph{right} orange'' is \hl{ignored}.
    \item Target switching only works when the arm is \hl{far from all oranges}.
    \item[$\Rightarrow$] Before any target change, the arm must \hl{first move away}.
  \end{itemize}
  \end{block}
  \vspace{0.4em}
  A \hl{dataset-coverage} effect --- target switching was only ever demonstrated from high, far-from-the-oranges configurations --- and exactly why the orchestrator needs a \hl{scripted home-pose reset}, defined next.
\end{frame}

% ===== 11. Failures vs timeouts =====
\begin{frame}{Two things to detect: failures vs.\ timeouts}
  \textbf{Physical failure} --- the orange is actually lost:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{LIFT} --- slips before reaching the plate.\quad \textbf{PLACE} --- dropped outside the plate.
    \item \textbf{GRASP} --- no instantaneous event: the gripper simply never closes on it.
  \end{itemize}
  \vspace{0.3em}
  \textbf{Timeout} --- a per-subtask step budget (\hl{only exists with the orchestrator}):
  \begin{itemize}
    \item A \hl{detection trigger} to react (retry / reset / redirect) --- \emph{not} a failure itself.
    \item Monotask has no timeout $\Rightarrow$ a failure is \hl{never caught mid-episode}.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

% ===== 12. Orchestrator: three mechanisms (+ figure) =====
\begin{frame}{Orchestrator: a finite-state controller around the VLA}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \begin{column}{0.56\textwidth}
      The VLA does \emph{only} GRASP / LIFT / PLACE; all checks use privileged state.
      \medskip

      Recovery at \hl{two levels} + one precondition:
      \begin{itemize}
        \item \hl{Level-1 --- local retry} \emph{(recovery)}: slip / timeout on LIFT or PLACE $\rightarrow$ re-GRASP the \textbf{same} orange.
        \item \hl{Level-2 --- target redirection} \emph{(recovery)}: GRASP timeout $\rightarrow$ switch to a \textbf{different} orange so the episode keeps scoring. \emph{Not} giving up --- task-level recovery.
        \item \hl{Home reset --- precondition} \emph{(not recovery)}: scripted move home before any target change.
      \end{itemize}
    \end{column}
    \begin{column}{0.44\textwidth}
      \centering
      \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{orchestrator_decision_flow.pdf}\\
      {\footnotesize Orchestrated decision flow.}
    \end{column}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

% ===== 13. Autonomous data generation =====
\begin{frame}{Probe: is the subtask policy just data-limited?}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Run the orchestrator \hl{on its own}; keep only the \hl{successful} subtasks, discard the failed ones.
    \item \hl{Target redirection} salvages data from scenes that would otherwise be wasted.
    \item \hl{+414 Auto} episodes merged with 846 Teleop $\rightarrow$ \hl{1260} (\emph{Teleop+Auto}).
    \item Auto episodes are \hl{shorter / less dispersed} --- drawn from the policy's own visited states.
  \end{itemize}
  \vfill
  {\footnotesize Question: does adding in-distribution successful data improve the orchestrated policy?}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Results: full fine-tuning is decisive --- and it is the vision encoder}

% ===== 16. Results: grasp bottleneck =====
\begin{frame}{Grasp acquisition is the limiting step}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Only $\sim$\hl{37\%} of grasp attempts secure an orange under standard fine-tuning (\gn{55\%} fully-tuned).
    \item Everything downstream is reliable: LIFT and PLACE succeed \gn{80--92\%}; once held, an orange reaches the plate \gn{87\%} of the time (Teleop).
    \item[$\Rightarrow$] The cap on full-task success is \hl{upstream}, at acquiring the orange at all.
  \end{itemize}
  \vfill
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{failure_modes.pdf}
\end{frame}

% ===== 17. Results: recovery at matched stalls =====
\begin{frame}{Results: recovery, compared at matched stalls}
  A \hl{stall} = one grasp budget of fruitless commitment to a single orange, defined identically for both formulations (monotask intent read from \emph{proximity}). What happens after?
  \vfill
  \centering\small
  \begin{tabular}{llccc}
    \toprule
    Training & Form. & Redirected & Placed after & \dots via \emph{different} orange \\
    \midrule
    Teleop      & Monotask          & 53.5\%     & 31.4\%        & 16.3\% \\
    Teleop      & \textbf{Subtask}  & \gn{100\%} & \gn{71.2\%}   & \gn{53.4\%} \\
    \midrule
    Teleop+Auto & Monotask          & 45.2\%     & 34.4\%        & 17.2\% \\
    Teleop+Auto & \textbf{Subtask}  & \gn{100\%} & \gn{43.2\%}   & \gn{33.7\%} \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
  \vfill
  \begin{itemize}
    \item The orchestrator redirects \hl{every} time (Level-2 by design); the monotask switches in only \hl{about half} the stalls --- and nothing ties its switch to the failure.
    \item The recovery gap sits \hl{entirely in the different-orange column}; same-orange recovery is formulation-independent (10--18\%). Level-1 retry stays marginal (6--11\% of placed oranges).
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

% ===== 18. Results: obedience =====
\begin{frame}{Results: does it grasp the orange it was told to?}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \begin{column}{0.46\textwidth}
      \begin{itemize}
        \item Only \hl{64--73\%} of grasps obey the requested label.
        \item \hl{Training more of the VLM does not help}: partial 67\%, full 64\% --- the lowest, despite being the strongest models overall.
        \item Misses are systematically \hl{leftward}: ``right'' is the worst-followed label (27--47\%).
        \item ``Bottom right'' is taken for top right in 29--62\% of cases --- its supervision is scarce and inconsistently labelled.
      \end{itemize}

% ===== 19. Discussion & limitations =====
\begin{frame}{Discussion \& limitations}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Decomposition creates \hl{real intervention points}: the system almost never fails completely --- the mechanism is Level-2 redirection.
    \item But it \hl{cannot fix a weak GRASP policy} --- the binding constraint sits \emph{before} any check or recovery can help.
    \item What moved the bottleneck was a \hl{stronger policy} (full fine-tuning, vision included), not more structure.
    \item Auto data \hl{helped one formulation and hurt the other} --- why is left open. More self-generated data is unlikely the fix: a near-deterministic policy filtered by a binary success bit cannot discover the grasp it is missing.
    \item Success checks use \hl{privileged state} --- deployment needs perception; 100 episodes per model, so small differences may be sampling noise.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

% ===== 20. Conclusion =====
\begin{frame}{Conclusion \& future work}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Orchestration makes failure \hl{gradual instead of total}: abandon an ungraspable orange, still place another.
    \item It did not reliably raise \hl{full completion}: the hard step --- grasping --- precedes every checkpoint.
    \item The two levers are \hl{complementary}: better grasping raises how far each attempt gets; orchestration catches what still fails.
    \item This task leaves a controller little to decide --- the orchestration shows its \hl{floor} here, not its ceiling.
    \item \textbf{Next:} explicit exploration (RL fine-tuning) or targeted demos for the hard grasps; \hl{from-any-state} subtask data; a \hl{VLM success monitor} to replace privileged checks.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}
