\section{Results}
\label{sec:experiments}

This section compares task formulations and training sources. Recall the dataset names: \textbf{LightwheelAI Baseline} (60 public end-to-end demonstrations), \textbf{Teleop} (manually collected subtasks), \textbf{Auto} (balanced auto-generated successful subtasks), and \textbf{Teleop+Auto} (their merge).

Every completed run uses the same 100-scene reference seed set (\texttt{pick\_orange\_reference\_100\_v1}) in the same order; the randomised quantities are the top-camera pose and the orange positions. Differences between models are therefore attributable to policy and formulation rather than to easier or harder sampled scenes.

\subsection{Overall Performance}

Every model is evaluated on the same 100 seeded scenes with a 5000-step budget. Two numbers summarise the outcomes: the \emph{full-task success rate} (share of episodes that end with all three oranges in the plate) and the \emph{mean orange count} (average number placed, so partial progress counts). Unless stated otherwise, a model uses the default SmolVLA fine-tuning of \cref{subsec:finetuning}, where only the action expert and the state projection are trained --- called \emph{standard} below. The first figure compares training sources and task formulations (\emph{monotask} end-to-end vs.\ orchestrated \emph{subtask}) under standard fine-tuning; a second figure at the end of this subsection adds three variants: \emph{LM-tuned}, where the language model of the VLM is trained as well; \emph{fully-tuned}, where the vision encoder is additionally unfrozen (Teleop subtask only, \cref{subsec:finetuning}); and \emph{no-tail}, where the terminal freeze frames are removed from the training data.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.88\textwidth]{orange_outcome_standard.pdf}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{ACT vs.\ SmolVLA.} The LightwheelAI Baseline is where the two policy types are compared: ACT, a standard imitation-learning baseline, against SmolVLA, a state-of-the-art VLA in the small-model category. Run end-to-end under a single instruction, SmolVLA places all three oranges in 41\% of episodes against 13\% for ACT, averages 1.95 oranges against 0.92, and leaves only 9\% of episodes empty against 53\%. SmolVLA on this dataset is also the best full-task result under standard fine-tuning, probably owing to the quality of the demonstrations: their motions are smooth, fast, and precise. Even so, 41\% is far from reliable, which is what the subtask decomposition aims to improve.

\paragraph{Decomposition helps partial progress more than full-task completion.} On the same trajectories, the subtask formulation's benefit is mainly in partial progress. On Teleop it raises mean occupancy sharply (1.02 to 1.77) while raising full-task success only modestly (14\% to 20\%): checkpoints convert empty and one-orange episodes into two-orange ones, rarely into three. On Teleop+Auto the picture is mixed --- the monotask model is ahead on full-task success (18\% vs.\ 13\%) but behind on mean occupancy (1.29 vs.\ 1.42). Decomposition thus raises mean occupancy on Teleop, but its effect on full-task completion is small and inconsistent across sources.

\paragraph{Subtasking rarely fails completely.} The clearest advantage is on the weakest outcome --- an episode with no orange placed. The subtask models almost always place at least one (97\% of Teleop episodes, 84\% on Teleop+Auto), whereas the monotask models leave far more empty (only 56\% and 64\% reach one). Target redirection is the likely reason --- but not because the monotask fixates on a single orange: it does attempt other oranges on its own (\cref{sec:failure_modes}), yet, lacking a deliberate give-up-and-redirect, it often exhausts the budget without securing any, while the controller detects a repeated GRASP timeout and redirects to another reachable orange. The decomposed formulation thus rarely fails totally, even when it does not complete the task.

\paragraph{Autonomous data helped the monotask but hurt the subtask model.} Adding the Auto episodes to Teleop improved the monotask model (full-task 14\% to 18\%, mean 1.02 to 1.29) yet regressed the subtask model on both metrics (20\% to 13\%, 1.77 to 1.42) --- surprising, since the extra episodes were collected in the orchestrated setting the subtask model runs in. In-distribution successful subtasks were thus not sufficient to improve the orchestrated behaviour; the subtask-level analysis below isolates what degraded, and \cref{sec:discussion} returns to why the same data may help one formulation and hurt the other.

\paragraph{Partial backbone tuning is inconsistent; the full recipe is decisive.} The figure below adds the three fine-tuning variants. \emph{LM-tuned} helps two configurations and hurts two: full-task success rises on the Teleop subtask model (20\% to 33\%, mean 1.77 to 1.88) and the Teleop+Auto monotask model (18\% to 25\%, 1.29 to 1.54), but falls on the Teleop monotask model (14\% to 9\%, 1.02 to 0.92) and the Teleop+Auto subtask model (13\% to 10\%, 1.42 to 1.37). The direction depends on source and formulation, and \cref{sec:obedience} shows the LM-tuned models do not follow their target instruction any better either. The \emph{no-tail} variant --- a pure data change, keeping standard fine-tuning --- gives the Teleop subtask model a similar gain (20\% to 32\%), so the LM-tuned improvement there is not clearly an effect of the extra trainable weights. The \emph{fully-tuned} variant, by contrast, is unambiguous: it reaches \textbf{58\%} full-task success with a mean of 2.32 oranges --- the best result in the whole comparison, well above the 41\% LightwheelAI baseline --- and it gets there by grasping better (per-attempt grasp success 37\% to 55\%, \cref{sec:failure_modes}). One caveat qualifies the size of the jump: for memory reasons the fully-tuned model trained with half the batch size and twice the gradient steps of every other model --- the same samples seen, but twice as many weight updates (\cref{subsec:limitations}).

\subsection{Failure Analysis}

The overview above reports \emph{what} each model achieves; this section digs into \emph{why} --- where execution breaks down and which step caps performance.

\subsubsection{Grasp Acquisition and Recovery Behaviour}
\label{sec:failure_modes}

\textbf{Grasp acquisition is the limiting step.} For the orchestrated (subtask) models --- the only ones whose grasp attempts are real, budgeted events rather than a heuristic reconstruction --- only about \textbf{37\%} of grasp attempts secure an orange under standard fine-tuning; the rest time out without acquiring one. The fully-tuned variant lifts this to 55\%, which is where its higher completion rate comes from. Everything downstream is reliable: lift and place each succeed 80--92\% of the time, so once an orange is held it reaches the plate \textbf{87\%} of the time on Teleop. The cap on full-task success is upstream, at acquiring the orange at all.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{failure_modes.pdf}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Recovery is compared at matched stalls.} If \textbf{Level-2} recovery works, then whenever the policy is stuck on an orange it cannot grasp, the orchestrated formulation should escape --- switch to another orange and keep scoring --- where the monotask policy, which has no such mechanism, should stay stuck. Comparing the two requires knowing, on both sides, which orange the policy is working on and when that effort has failed. For the subtask both are explicit: the target is named in the GRASP prompt, and failure is the prompt timing out after its 700-step budget. For the monotask neither is observable --- no prompt names a target --- so its intent is approximated by \emph{proximity}: the orange it is working on is taken to be the nearest not-yet-placed orange to the gripper. A \textbf{stall} is then defined for both formulations as one grasp budget of consecutive commitment to a single orange without a force-confirmed grasp ($\geq$10\,N on both fingertips): for the subtask, exactly the first timed-out GRASP prompt on a target; for the monotask, 600 consecutive steps nearest to the same orange with nothing secured. The lower threshold compensates the proximity clock starting late --- an orange only becomes nearest partway into an approach, roughly 130--150 steps in, judging by successful acquisitions (median 286--290 steps from the home pose vs.\ 140--170 once nearest) --- and no conclusion below changes for any threshold between 500 and 700. Two filters apply identically to both formulations: a stall counts only if another orange is still unplaced, so redirection is possible, and only if at least 1500 of the 5000 episode steps remain --- more than the median time to the next placement, so recovery is not cut short by the episode ending. The table then follows each stall: was the next orange worked on a \emph{different} one (\emph{redirected}), was any orange placed afterwards --- split by whether that first placement was the \emph{same} committed orange or a \emph{different} one --- and how many steps it took.

\textbf{After a stall, the orchestrated formulation recovers more often and sooner.} Reading \cref{tab:level2_deadline} column by column:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep, topsep=2pt, leftmargin=1.6em]
    \item \emph{Redirected} --- the two formulations first respond differently. The orchestrator redirects \emph{every} time: Level-2 recovery firing by design, spending at most one grasp budget on a failing orange before switching. The monotask policy switches away in only about half the cases, as expected --- it is trained on perfect demonstrations, where no grasp fails and hence no target is ever abandoned; redirection is a response to failure, and failure is absent from the training data.
    \item \emph{Placed an orange after} --- the central result. After the same fruitless effort on one orange, the orchestrated formulation goes on to place an orange far more often on Teleop (71\% of stalls against 31\%) and somewhat more often on Teleop+Auto (43\% against 34\%).
    \item \emph{Same vs.\ different} --- the gain is tied to redirection. Recovery through the \emph{same} orange is formulation-independent at 10--18\% of stalls (for the orchestrated formulation, which never persists past a stall, these are the minority of cases where a later redirection led \emph{back} to the abandoned orange); the entire gap sits in the \emph{different-orange} column, 53\% vs.\ 16\% on Teleop and 34\% vs.\ 17\% on Teleop+Auto. Re-grasping the \emph{same} orange after a slip --- \textbf{Level-1} recovery --- remains marginal (only 6--11\% of placed oranges took more than one grasp).
    \item \emph{Median steps} --- the orchestrated formulation also tends to recover sooner (median 1030 vs.\ 1440 steps on Teleop), but the step counts carry less weight than the rates: the monotask stall rests on the approximate 600-step proximity threshold, so the timing lead is indicative rather than exact.
\end{itemize}
Only the redirection \emph{behaviour} itself is attributed to the recovery logic; the conversion rates are outcomes, to which the policy's per-attempt grasp quality also contributes.

\subsubsection{Grasp Obedience}
\label{sec:obedience}

Only the orchestrated runs name their target by spatial label (``Grasp \emph{<position>} orange''), so only they can be asked whether the policy grasps the orange it was \emph{told} to --- a grasp is \textbf{obeyed} when the gripper closes on the requested orange and a \textbf{misgrab} otherwise. The subtask policies obey only about \textbf{two-thirds to three-quarters} of the time (64--73\%), and \textbf{training more of the VLM does not help}: the LM-tuned variants obey 67\% on both sources and the fully-tuned variant 64\% --- the lowest of all, despite being the strongest model overall. Better grasping does not bring better instruction following.

\textbf{The misses are systematically leftward.} On the dominant left--right axis a missed request lands on the orange just to the \emph{left} of the one asked for (\emph{right} taken as \emph{middle}, \emph{middle} as \emph{left}), toward the side where the plate sits. So \emph{right} is the worst-followed label (27--47\%), while the leftmost and upper referents are almost always obeyed (84--100\%, one outlier at 71\%). It is genuine behaviour, not a logging artefact: the grasped orange is identified from contact, a physically distinct one a few centimetres to the side of the one requested.

\textbf{The two vertical labels blur into one another} --- and asymmetrically: a \emph{bottom right} request lands on the top-right orange in 29--62\% of cases, while \emph{top right} is almost never taken for \emph{bottom right} (0--9\%). These composite labels arise only when two oranges sit within 3\,cm laterally and must be told apart along the perpendicular axis --- and they are exactly the labels whose training supervision is scarcest and least reliably bounded (\cref{subsec:limitations}).
