\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

This work studies orchestrated manipulation in a simulated pick-and-place task. The robot is an SO-101 arm operating in an Isaac Sim kitchen scene. Each episode contains three oranges and a plate. The objective is to place all three oranges into the plate.

At every step the policy observes the same fixed inputs: the six SO-101 joint states and two RGB streams (a fixed top camera and a wrist camera). From these alone it predicts the next chunk of joint actions; it has no access to object coordinates or any privileged simulator quantity. Privileged state (orange and plate poses, contact forces) is used \emph{only} by the orchestrator and the evaluation harness to judge whether a subtask achieved its physical outcome. Withholding it from the policy avoids confounding orchestration with visual state-estimation errors, while exposing it to the orchestrator means subtask success is measured directly rather than inferred from perception.

\subsection{Policy Architecture and Fine-Tuning}
\label{subsec:finetuning}

The policy used throughout is \textbf{SmolVLA}~\cite{shukor2025smolvla}, a compact Vision-Language-Action model. It couples a pretrained vision-language backbone --- the \num{500}M-parameter \texttt{SmolVLM2-500M-Video-Instruct} (a vision encoder and a 16-layer language transformer) --- with a smaller \textbf{action expert}. The backbone encodes the two camera views and the prompt into tokens; the action expert cross-attends to them and, conditioned on the current joint state, denoises a noise sample into the next action chunk via a flow-matching objective, with a linear \textbf{state projection} mapping the six-dimensional joint state into token space. Each query outputs a chunk of 20 six-dimensional joint actions, matching the 20-frame terminal hold used in data collection (\cref{subsec:teleop}). All models start from the public \texttt{lerobot/smolvla\_base} checkpoint.

Fine-tuning is \textbf{parameter-efficient}: the vision encoder and the language backbone are frozen and only the \textbf{action expert} and \textbf{state projection} are updated --- roughly \num{100}M of the model's \num{450}M parameters (about 22\%) --- keeping the pretrained visual and linguistic priors intact while adapting the action pathway to the SO-101. Optimisation uses AdamW (learning rate \num{1e-4}, $\beta=(0.9,0.95)$, weight decay \num{1e-10}) on a cosine schedule with 1000-step warm-up, batch size 64, and \num{20000} gradient steps; the joint state and actions are mean/standard-deviation normalised and images identity-scaled. \Cref{fig:smolvla_finetune} summarises which components are frozen and which are trained.

As a separate ablation we \textbf{unfreeze the language backbone}, training the 16-layer transformer jointly with the action expert (the vision encoder stays frozen). This raises the trainable count from \num{100}M to \num{316}M (about 70\%) and lets the backbone adapt to the robot domain rather than reusing web-pretrained features, at the cost of a heavier optimisation more prone to overfitting a single dataset. It is trained on the same Teleop subtask data and evaluated under the identical protocol, so any difference is attributable to the unfreezing alone. On the 100-episode seeded benchmark of \cref{sec:experiments}, unfreezing raises full-task success (all three oranges placed) from 20\% to 33\% and the mean oranges placed from 1.77 to 1.88, while reaching success in fewer steps (2793 vs.\ 3043); the gain is concentrated in placement reliability, at the cost of a heavier tail of complete failures (9 vs.\ 3 zero-orange episodes). Unfreezing the backbone therefore helps on this single dataset, though the heavier optimisation makes the worst case more variable.

\subsection{LightwheelAI Baseline}

The first setting treats the task as a single full-task imitation problem. The \textbf{LightwheelAI Baseline} is the 60-episode \texttt{leisaac-pick-orange-mimic-v0} dataset published by LightwheelAI (developers of the upstream \texttt{leisaac} library this project builds on), the official reference for the task. Each of its 60 demonstrations covers the full three-orange sequence and is unsegmented --- not split into subtasks, with the policy expected to execute the whole task end-to-end.

Two policy classes are compared: ACT~\cite{zhao2023act}, an action-chunking transformer, and SmolVLA. Both are trained from the same 60-demonstration source under a single fixed instruction, so the comparison isolates the policy backbone rather than language conditioning. \textbf{[Placeholder: motivation for the ACT vs.\ SmolVLA comparison to be finalised.]} SmolVLA is used throughout the rest of the work. This experiment also establishes a performance ceiling for the \textbf{monotask} formulation --- the whole task run end-to-end under one prompt, without subtask checkpoints --- which we contrast throughout with the orchestrated \textbf{subtask} formulation: if even the stronger policy cannot solve the task reliably here, the limit is unlikely to be the low-level policy's expressiveness alone, motivating the decomposition introduced next.

\subsection{Subtask Decomposition}

Detecting and responding to intermediate failures requires knowing when a behaviour has ended and whether it succeeded. The full task is therefore reformulated as a sequence of subtasks --- grasping a selected orange, lifting it, and placing it --- creating explicit checkpoints where the expected physical outcome can be verified before committing to the next step.

Targeted grasp prompts use relative spatial labels from the current layout: left, middle, right in simple configurations. When two oranges are too close along the main horizontal axis to separate by left/right alone, the secondary axis breaks the tie (labels such as top, bottom, top right, bottom left), keeping the language target meaningful even when oranges are laterally clustered.

An intermediate attempt sliced the LightwheelAI Baseline into subtask segments, but its demonstrations always follow the same object order, so target identity is entangled with progress through the fixed sequence rather than tied to the instruction. This motivated collecting a custom dataset.

\subsection{Teleop Dataset Construction}
\label{subsec:teleop}

A custom teleoperated dataset, referred to as \textbf{Teleop}, was collected to support orchestrated execution, with the SO-101 arm driven by hand through an Xbox gamepad controller. Each episode is a single successful subtask annotated with its instruction, such as ``Grasp left orange'', ``Grasp top right orange'', ``Pick it up'', or ``Place it into plate''. The pick order is randomised across episodes, which breaks the fixed-order correlation of the baseline and forces the policy to associate a relative-position instruction with the specified orange rather than inferring the target from task progress. The dataset used for the evaluated subtask model contains 846 such episodes.

The teleoperated motions are markedly slower than the baseline: precise fast control is hard with the gamepad, so the operator moved deliberately. The trajectories therefore differ from the LightwheelAI Baseline in velocity profile, not only in subtask-level segmentation.

A key design choice is \textbf{tailing}: every episode is extended with 20 terminal freeze frames (one action chunk), repeating the final observation and state and converting the command into a hold once the subtask completes. This teaches the policy to \textbf{stay idle at a subtask boundary} instead of flowing into the next phase, which serves three roles. It lets a manually-driven operator pause and think while the arm holds its pose and keeps its grasp instead of drifting or dropping the orange. More importantly, it makes the policy robust to an orchestrator that does not advance instantaneously --- e.g.\ a higher-latency external vision-based controller --- since it holds rather than drifts while waiting. And because a finished subtask manifests as the arm coming to rest, the tail gives a natural completion signal a higher-level controller can detect. The resulting SmolVLA model is thus a subtask executor: on reaching a terminal configuration it settles and waits rather than continuing to act.

\subsection{Orchestrator and Failure Modes}
\label{subsec:orchestrator}
\label{subsec:failure_taxonomy}

The orchestrator is a finite-state controller that wraps the VLA and issues only three prompts --- GRASP(\textit{target}), LIFT, PLACE. It selects a target, sends the prompt, reads the physical outcome from privileged state, and decides whether to advance, retry, redirect, or terminate. \Cref{fig:orchestrator_flow} gives the full transition logic and the per-subtask success checks; we describe only the high-level design here. Reading outcomes from privileged state means the experiment evaluates the control structure, not visual state estimation.

    \caption{Decision logic of the orchestrator. The VLA runs only the manipulation subtasks; target selection, retries, redirection, and the home-pose reset are handled by the controller.}
    \label{fig:orchestrator_flow}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Outcomes and failure modes.} Each subtask has one intended physical outcome, read directly from the simulator: a \textbf{grasp} acquires the target (gripper closed with confirmed contact), a \textbf{lift} raises the held orange clear of the table, a \textbf{place} brings it to rest inside the plate. A failure is the negation of that check, and all three are well defined for any rollout independent of task framing --- the property that lets monotask and subtask runs be scored on the same footing (the monotask, having no online checkpoints, has these events reconstructed offline from the same privileged state). A subtask is declared failed when its check is unmet within a fixed step budget, so the timeout is a \emph{detection trigger}, not a fourth failure mode: GRASP can only fail by timeout, whereas LIFT and PLACE can also fail the instant the orange is lost. These budgets, and the hand-designed state machine itself, are pragmatic heuristics rather than optimised choices --- a limitation we return to in \cref{sec:discussion}.

\textbf{Recovery is defined at two levels of the goal hierarchy.} \textbf{Level-1} (local retry) recovers the \emph{same} orange: after a LIFT or PLACE drop or timeout the controller keeps the target and returns to GRASP. \textbf{Level-2} (target redirection) recovers task \emph{progress}: after a GRASP timeout it switches to a different reachable orange so the episode keeps scoring, falling back to a Level-1 retry only if none remains. Because the objective is indifferent to \emph{which} orange is placed, redirection serves the task rather than abandoning it. This split also makes the recovery metrics fair across formulations: a LIFT drop or PLACE slip happens to an orange that was demonstrably held, so ``the same orange'' is unambiguous (Level-1), whereas a GRASP failure never establishes contact and so has no recoverable identity --- Level-2 only asks whether a \emph{different} orange is later placed, needing no inferred target.

\textbf{Home-pose reset is a precondition, not a recovery level.} Before any Level-2 target change the controller scripts the arm back to a \emph{high, far-from-the-oranges} pose. This is needed because of how the data was collected, not a property of VLAs: such poses occur only at episode start and just after a placement, and there the dataset shows the same pose branching to different oranges by instruction --- the only region where the policy learned to let language pick the target. Once the gripper is low and near an orange, grasps were demonstrated one target at a time, so no redirection branch exists and a new prompt is ignored (quantified in \cref{sec:obedience}). Such redirection demonstrations would also have to be filtered out of the shared monotask data, so the scripted reset is the pragmatic alternative. A Level-1 retry on the same target needs no reset.

The monotask formulation has no such structure: the policy runs end-to-end under one global prompt within a single 5000-step budget, with no checkpoints and no per-subtask timeout. The same three failure modes occur but are never detected or acted on online. For a common footing, the analysis in \cref{sec:experiments} reconstructs the same physical events for monotask runs offline from privileged state (inferring the worked target from the layout), so grasp, lift, and place outcomes are readable for both formulations even though only the orchestrated one observes them online.

\subsection{Autonomous Data Generation}

Early evaluation of the Teleop subtask model identified GRASP as the dominant bottleneck: lifting and placing were reliable once an orange was held, but the policy could not acquire certain oranges at all, depending on their randomly initialised position. Recovery therefore ran mainly through \textbf{Level-2} redirection --- abandoning an ungraspable orange for a reachable one --- rather than \textbf{Level-1} retries, which follow only the rarer post-grasp slip. We hypothesised that this reflected a \emph{coverage} gap: the policy generalised poorly across orange positions and simply needed more grasp data. Because teleoperation is slow to scale, we used the orchestrated system to generate that data automatically. (Whether more data helps is tested in \cref{sec:experiments}; it does not --- the limit turns out to be instruction \emph{grounding}, not coverage.)

\textbf{The loop.} A rollout contains both failed and successful subtasks; the system keeps the successful trajectories --- drawn from the policy's own execution distribution --- and discards the rest, yielding the \textbf{Auto} set. Target redirection is essential here: by pivoting to a different orange after a GRASP timeout instead of discarding the whole rollout, the system keeps harvesting successful subtasks from scenes that would otherwise be wasted.

\textbf{A reinforcement-learning reading.} This filter-and-retrain loop is a minimal form of reinforcement learning: the orchestrator's privileged success check is a sparse binary reward, and fine-tuning on the kept trajectories is reward-weighted behavioural cloning with the success indicator as the weight. With a single bit per subtask it does no within-trajectory credit assignment and runs for a single update round --- the crudest policy improvement from self-generated experience, chosen precisely because it reuses the existing pipeline unchanged and tests whether the cheapest possible data loop helps before any more elaborate scheme is justified.

The retained episodes are balanced by scene state --- equalising start-of-episode scenes against those with one or two oranges already placed --- so the set does not over-represent early scenes. The 414 retained Auto episodes were merged with the 846 Teleop episodes into \textbf{Teleop+Auto} (1260 episodes), used to train the Teleop+Auto subtask model.

Two features are relevant to later results. GRASP dominates the length budget --- two to three times longer than LIFT or PLACE, with by far the heaviest tail --- because it includes the variable search-and-approach phase before contact. The Auto episodes are shorter and less dispersed than their Teleop counterparts, consistent with being drawn from the policy's own visited states rather than human demonstration.

\subsection{Monotask Fair Comparison}

A subtask model differs from the LightwheelAI Baseline in both training distribution and formulation, so that comparison cannot isolate subtasking. We therefore pair each subtask model with a \textbf{monotask} counterpart trained on the \emph{same} trajectories: per-subtask instructions replaced by the single fixed prompt ``Place the orange into plate'', freeze tails removed, run end-to-end without the orchestrator's checkpoints. The pair sees identical demonstrations and differs only in framing, at both training and execution time; every run uses the same 5000-step budget.

The match is in data but not temporal supervision --- intentionally, since supervision is part of the formulation under test: the freeze tails teach the subtask model to settle at each boundary, while the monotask model never sees boundaries and keeps acting under one prompt. The results thus compare two formulations, not a single controlled factor.

A further caveat: neither model is trained on a genuine end-to-end three-orange trajectory. The monotask data is itself reused subtask fragments under one prompt, not continuous runs, and the Teleop subtasks were curated for quality in isolation --- a coverage gap discussed in \cref{sec:discussion}.
