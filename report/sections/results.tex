\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{Decomposition broadened partial progress, not completion.} The hypothesis (\cref{sec:introduction}) was that wrapping the policy in subtask checkpoints with two-level recovery would make the long-horizon task more robust. The realised payoff is narrower: the orchestrated system almost never fails \emph{completely}, placing at least one orange in 97\% of Teleop episodes and 84\% on Teleop+Auto, against 56\% and 64\% for the monolithic policy. The mechanism is \textbf{Level-2} redirection --- giving up on an ungraspable orange to try another, about 2.7 distinct oranges per episode, against about 2.0--2.2 the end-to-end policy reaches only incidentally and far less effectively (\cref{sec:failure_modes}). It did not raise full-task completion: the best subtask model places all three oranges in only 20/100 episodes, below the 41/100 LightwheelAI baseline, and on Teleop+Auto the monolithic model leads on full-task success (18\% vs.\ 13\%) but not on mean occupancy (1.29 vs.\ 1.42). Decomposition secures more oranges on average, not more finished tasks.

\textbf{The grasp is the only hard step, and decomposition does not make it easier.} Once an orange is held the rest is reliable: lift and place each succeed 80--92\% of the time, and a secured orange reaches the plate 73--87\% of the time. The limiting step is acquisition --- only about 37\% of grasp attempts secure an orange, and the policy grasps the \emph{requested} orange just 67--73\% of the time, worst on \emph{right}, the label the task produces most (\cref{sec:obedience}). The orchestrator's checkpoints sit at boundaries that are already dependable; no amount of monitoring places an orange the policy never grasps. The three-way GRASP/LIFT/PLACE split is thus finer than the failure structure warrants: \textbf{Level-1} local retry almost never decides an outcome --- only 12 of 186 placed oranges took more than one grasp --- so the realised contribution collapses onto Level-2 redirection alone, itself capped by how often a reachable alternative remains.

\textbf{Autonomous data helped one formulation and hurt the other.} Adding 414 self-generated successful subtasks \emph{helped} the monolithic policy (full-task 14\% to 18\%, mean 1.02 to 1.29, empty episodes 44\% to 36\%) yet \emph{regressed} the orchestrated one (20\% to 13\%, mean 1.77 to 1.42, secured oranges per episode 2.13 to 1.94, a held orange placed 87\% down to 73\%). Why the same data helps one formulation and hurts the other is left open. One reading is that subtasking's advantage is redirection, which pays off only when the policy gets stuck: if the extra data made grasping more reliable, redirection would matter less and the formulation's minor overheads would dominate. Two observations keep this unresolved. First, a monolithic per-attempt grasp \emph{rate} is still not measurable, though its secured-per-episode does rise (1.26 to 1.47) while the distinct oranges it approaches falls slightly (2.2 to 2.0, \cref{sec:failure_modes}) --- weakly consistent with ``gets stuck less.'' Second, the orchestrated policy still fails completely far less often on Teleop+Auto (16\% vs.\ 36\% empty), so redirection is plainly still active --- arguing against the idea that it simply lost its purpose.

\textbf{More self-generated data is unlikely to be the fix.} The autonomous loop is a weak improvement mechanism by construction. SmolVLA produces each action chunk by integrating a flow-matching field from a Gaussian noise sample (\cref{subsec:finetuning}); trained by behavioural cloning on near-deterministic teleoperation, it largely reproduces the demonstrated motion rather than exploring alternative grasps. The harvested episodes are correspondingly narrow --- shorter and less dispersed than their Teleop counterparts (\cref{fig:dataset_composition}) --- a self-distribution that reinforces what the policy already does well. The loop also assigns a single success bit to a whole subtask with no within-chunk credit, a reward-weighted behavioural-cloning round rather than genuine policy search (\cref{subsec:autonomous}). A near-deterministic policy filtered by a coarse binary reward cannot discover the grasp it is missing; closing the acquisition gap likely needs explicit exploration --- for instance online reinforcement fine-tuning of the flow policy --- or targeted demonstrations on the hard \emph{right} and late-scene grasps, not more in-distribution data.

\textbf{Cheaper recipe tweaks give no reliable gain.} Two alternatives to new data were tried. Unfreezing the language backbone (\cref{subsec:finetuning}) clearly helps only the Teleop subtask model (full-task 20\% to 33\%, mean 1.77 to 1.88); on the other three formulations the change is mixed and within sampling noise (Teleop monotask 14\% to 9\%, Teleop+Auto subtask 13\% to 10\%, Teleop+Auto monotask 18\% to 25\%). Removing the terminal freeze frames (\cref{subsec:teleop}), tried on the Teleop subtask alone, reaches 32\%/1.81 --- essentially the unfrozen result. Because each action chunk is sampled from a fresh noise seed, differences of a few points over 100 episodes may reflect sampling noise rather than a real ordering; the one robust effect is that unfreezing helps the Teleop subtask, and none of these variants approaches the 41\% monolithic baseline.

\subsection{Conclusion}

This project frames recovery as an execution-level requirement for VLA manipulation and separates it into two levels --- sub-goal recovery by Level-1 local retry and task recovery by Level-2 redirection --- wrapped around a SmolVLA subtask executor, with a scripted spatial reset as the enabler that lets language switch targets. The realised value of decomposition is robustness against \emph{total} failure: redirection lets the system abandon an ungraspable orange and still score, so it rarely ends empty. It did not raise full-task completion, because the binding constraint --- reliably acquiring the \emph{requested} orange --- lies upstream of every checkpoint and is untouched by the surrounding structure; neither the recovery levels nor the autonomous data removes it.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{subsec:limitations}

\textbf{Privileged supervision and limited sampling.} Subtask outcomes are read from privileged simulator state, which a real deployment would have to replace with perception, and each evaluation covers only 100 seeded episodes, so the reported percentages are limited-sample estimates. The monolithic grasp trace is moreover reconstructed offline from that privileged state, so no monotask per-attempt grasp \emph{rate} is reported; per-step gripper--orange proximity logging recovers how many distinct oranges the policy approaches (\cref{sec:failure_modes}), but not a per-attempt rate.

\textbf{Hand-designed control.} The per-subtask step budgets and the finite-state machine itself are hand-set heuristics rather than optimised choices.

\textbf{Chronologically constrained subtask data.} The subtasks were collected as isolated fragments in their natural chronological order, and a subtask is never started from an arbitrary state --- for instance redirecting to a new orange while the gripper is still low and near another. A fully general atomistic policy would instead be trained to perform any subtask from any state, which would let it switch targets on its own and remove the need for the scripted spatial reset (\cref{subsec:failure_taxonomy}). This coverage was deliberately not collected so that the \emph{same} trajectories could serve the matched monotask comparison (\cref{subsec:monotask}): an end-to-end monotask run is only meaningful for chronologically ordered steps --- being on one orange and then leaving it for another makes no sense without checkpoints --- so from-arbitrary-state data would have broken that shared-dataset control. The cost is a less general policy that leans on the scripted reset and is not trained on complete, imperfect full-task trajectories, plausibly limiting long-horizon chaining and off-distribution robustness.

\subsection{Future Work}

The two levels of the system suggest two largely independent directions: making the atomistic subtask policy more capable, and generalising the orchestration around it.

\subsubsection{Subtask Execution}

The binding constraint --- grasp acquisition --- lies within the atomistic subtask policy, so making that policy more capable is the priority, and this is largely a matter of data. More demonstrations and higher-quality ones would help directly: the SO-101's five actuated arm joints are one short of the six degrees of freedom a full end-effector pose requires, which made hand-tracked teleoperation unintuitive and ultimately unusable (\cref{subsec:teleop}), so a 6-DOF arm would make teleoperation natural enough to collect a larger, more diverse, and cleaner dataset.

Quality also means \emph{coverage}. The data should deliberately include episodes that begin from states a normal run never reaches --- being told to grasp the right orange while already holding or hovering over another --- so the policy decorrelates its actions from apparent progress through the global task and obeys the instruction strictly instead of inferring the target from context (it currently follows the prompt only about two-thirds of the time, \cref{sec:obedience}). This is the from-any-state coverage the chronologically-ordered data lacks (\cref{subsec:limitations}); collecting it would make the policy a true from-any-state subtask executor and remove its reliance on the scripted spatial reset.

\subsubsection{Orchestration}

The high-level orchestration is largely orthogonal to the policy and can generalise along two axes. First, it currently reads each subtask's outcome from privileged simulator state, which is unavailable on a real robot (\cref{subsec:failure_taxonomy}); judging success from observations alone --- for instance with a vision-language model (VLM) as a success monitor --- would make the same control structure deployable in reality.

Second, the value of an explicit orchestrator should grow with task complexity. The present task is simple enough that a hand-designed finite-state machine suffices, but for long-horizon tasks with many object types, ordering constraints, and irreversible steps, enumerating and demonstrating every case end-to-end becomes infeasible --- exactly where a high-level controller earns its place. Given a reliable library of atomistic subtask policies, the decision of which subtask to run and when could be delegated either to a finite-state machine, when the task structure is known, or to an external language or vision-language model, when it is not, with the VLA optionally signalling subtask completion.
