\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Task and Environment}
\label{subsec:task}

This work studies orchestrated manipulation in a simulated pick-and-place task. The robot is an SO-101 arm operating in an Isaac Sim kitchen scene, built on the Isaac Lab framework~\cite{mittal2023orbit}. Each episode contains three oranges and a plate. The objective is to place all three oranges into the plate.

At every step the policy observes the same fixed inputs: the six SO-101 joint states and two RGB streams (a fixed top camera and a wrist camera). From these alone it predicts the next chunk of joint actions; it has no access to object coordinates or any privileged simulator quantity. Privileged state (orange and plate poses, contact forces) is used \emph{only} by the orchestrator and the evaluation harness to judge whether a subtask achieved its physical outcome. Withholding it from the policy avoids confounding orchestration with visual state-estimation errors, while exposing it to the orchestrator means subtask success is measured directly rather than inferred from perception.

\subsection{Policy Architecture and Fine-Tuning}
\label{subsec:finetuning}

The policy used throughout is \textbf{SmolVLA}~\cite{shukor2025smolvla}, a compact Vision-Language-Action model. It couples a pretrained vision-language backbone --- a trimmed \texttt{SmolVLM2-500M-Video-Instruct} (a vision encoder and a 16-layer language transformer; SmolVLA keeps only part of the source model's layers) --- with a smaller \textbf{action expert}. The backbone encodes the two camera views and the prompt into tokens; the action expert cross-attends to them and, conditioned on the current joint state, denoises a noise sample into the next action chunk via a flow-matching objective, with a linear \textbf{state projection} mapping the six-dimensional joint state into token space. Each query outputs a chunk of 20 six-dimensional joint actions, matching the 20-frame terminal hold used in data collection (\cref{subsec:teleop}). All models start from the public \texttt{lerobot/smolvla\_base} checkpoint.

Fine-tuning is \textbf{parameter-efficient}: the vision encoder and the language backbone are frozen and only the \textbf{action expert} and \textbf{state projection} are updated --- roughly \num{100}M of the model's \num{450}M parameters (about 22\%) --- keeping the pretrained visual and linguistic priors intact while adapting the action pathway to the SO-101. Optimisation uses AdamW (learning rate \num{1e-4}, $\beta=(0.9,0.95)$, weight decay \num{1e-10}) on a cosine schedule with 1000-step warm-up, batch size 64, and \num{20000} gradient steps; the joint state and actions are mean/standard-deviation normalised and images identity-scaled. \Cref{fig:smolvla_finetune} summarises which components are frozen and which are trained.

A separate ablation \textbf{also trains the language model}: the 16-layer transformer is fine-tuned jointly with the action expert (the vision encoder stays frozen). This variant is called \emph{LM-tuned} in the results; the default fine-tuning above is called \emph{standard}. LM-tuning raises the trainable count from \num{100}M to \num{316}M (about 70\%) and lets the language model adapt to the robot domain rather than reusing web-pretrained features, at the cost of a heavier optimisation more prone to overfitting a single dataset. Each of the four formulations is trained in both variants and evaluated under the identical protocol, so any difference is attributable to this change alone. A third recipe, \emph{fully-tuned}, unfreezes the vision encoder as well and trains all \num{450}M parameters; it exists for the Teleop subtask dataset only and, unlike every other model, trained for \num{40000} gradient steps instead of \num{20000}, so its results combine the wider unfreezing with a doubled training budget. The effects of all three recipes are examined in \cref{sec:discussion}.

\subsection{Task Decomposition}
\label{subsec:decomposition}

Detecting and responding to intermediate failures requires knowing when a behaviour has ended and whether it succeeded. The full task is therefore reformulated as a sequence of subtasks --- grasping a selected orange, lifting it, and placing it --- creating explicit checkpoints where the expected physical outcome can be verified before committing to the next step.

Targeted grasp prompts use relative spatial labels from the current layout: left, middle, right in simple configurations. When two oranges are too close along the main horizontal axis to separate by left/right alone, the secondary axis breaks the tie (labels such as top, bottom, top right, bottom left), keeping the language target meaningful even when oranges are laterally clustered.

\subsection{Orchestrator and Failure Modes}
\label{subsec:orchestrator}
\label{subsec:failure_taxonomy}

The orchestrator is a finite-state controller that wraps the VLA and issues only three prompts --- GRASP(\textit{target}), LIFT, PLACE. It selects a target, sends the prompt, reads the physical outcome from privileged state, and decides whether to advance, retry, redirect, or terminate. \Cref{fig:orchestrator_flow} gives the full transition logic and the per-subtask success checks; only the high-level design is described here. Reading outcomes from privileged state means the experiment evaluates the control structure, not visual state estimation.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{orchestrator_decision_flow.pdf}
    \caption{Decision logic of the orchestrator. The VLA runs only the manipulation subtasks; target selection, retries, redirection, and the home-pose reset are handled by the controller.}
    \label{fig:orchestrator_flow}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Outcomes and failure modes.} Each subtask has one intended physical outcome, read directly from the simulator: a \textbf{grasp} acquires the target (gripper closed with confirmed contact), a \textbf{lift} raises the held orange clear of the table, a \textbf{place} brings it to rest inside the plate. A failure is the negation of that check, and all three are well defined for any rollout independent of task framing --- the property that lets monotask and subtask runs be scored on the same footing (the monotask, having no online checkpoints, has these events reconstructed offline from the same privileged state). A subtask is declared failed when its check is unmet within a fixed step budget, so the timeout is a \emph{detection trigger}, not a fourth failure mode: GRASP can only fail by timeout, whereas LIFT and PLACE can also fail the instant the orange is lost. These budgets, and the hand-designed state machine itself, are pragmatic heuristics rather than optimised choices --- a limitation revisited in \cref{sec:discussion}.

\textbf{The orchestrator implements two recovery mechanisms.} \textbf{Level-1} (local retry) is a classic retry on the \emph{same} orange: when the orange is dropped or the check times out during LIFT or PLACE, the controller keeps the target and returns to GRASP. \textbf{Level-2} (target redirection) switches to a \emph{different} orange: when GRASP itself times out, the controller gives up on that orange and selects another reachable one, so the episode does not spend its remaining budget on an orange the policy cannot secure. Since the task counts oranges placed regardless of which, redirection keeps the episode progressing; the controller falls back to a Level-1 retry only when no other orange remains.

\textbf{The home-pose reset is the precondition for redirection.} Before any Level-2 target change the controller scripts the arm back to a \emph{high, far-from-the-oranges} pose. The reason is data coverage: only from such poses --- seen at episode start and just after a placement --- did the training data contain different continuations from the same state under different prompts, so this is the only region where the instruction carries a strong signal for the policy. Near an orange, every demonstration continues toward a single target whatever the prompt says, so a new instruction there is ignored (why the data has this shape is discussed in \cref{subsec:limitations}). A Level-1 retry on the same target needs no reset.

The monotask formulation has no such structure: the policy runs end-to-end under one global prompt within a single 5000-step budget, with no checkpoints and no per-subtask timeout. The same three failure modes occur but are never detected or acted on online. For a common footing, \cref{sec:experiments} reconstructs these events for monotask runs offline from privileged state, so subtask outcomes are readable for both formulations though only the orchestrated one observes them online.

\subsection{Datasets}
\label{subsec:datasets}

This work draws on three data sources: a public baseline of end-to-end demonstrations, and two custom sets --- one teleoperated, one autonomously generated --- usable in both the subtask and the monotask formulation. Each is described in turn, followed by their combined composition and the matched monotask data used for the controlled comparison (\cref{subsec:monotask}).

\subsubsection{Public Baseline Dataset}
\label{subsec:baseline}

The \textbf{LightwheelAI Baseline} is \texttt{leisaac-pick-orange-mimic-v0}: 60 public episodes released on the LeRobot~\cite{lerobot2024} Hub by LightwheelAI, the developers of the \texttt{leisaac} library this project builds on. It is adopted as the baseline because it is clean and openly available rather than collected by us. Each demonstration covers the whole three-orange sequence end-to-end, with smooth motions and no subtask segmentation.

This dataset serves two purposes. First, it hosts the policy comparison: ACT~\cite{zhao2023act}, a standard behavioural-cloning policy, and SmolVLA, the compact state-of-the-art VLA used in the rest of the work, are trained on the same 60 demonstrations under a single fixed instruction, so any performance difference is due to the policy class rather than the data or the language conditioning. Second, it introduces the \textbf{monotask} formulation --- the whole task run end-to-end under one prompt, without subtask checkpoints --- the reference against which the orchestrated \textbf{subtask} formulation is compared throughout. If even the stronger policy cannot solve the task reliably on these clean demonstrations, the limit is unlikely to be the policy class alone --- part of the motivation for the subtask decomposition (\cref{subsec:decomposition}).

\subsubsection{Teleoperation}
\label{subsec:teleop}

\textbf{Teleop} is a custom teleoperated dataset built to make language conditioning learnable. The public baseline always picks the oranges in the same order, so a policy trained on it can solve the task from state alone and never has to read the instruction --- it never sees the same configuration require a different orange depending on the prompt. Teleop breaks this by \textbf{randomising the pick order}. Each episode is one successful subtask, driven by hand with an Xbox gamepad and annotated with its instruction (``Grasp left orange'', ``Grasp top right orange'', ``Pick it up'', ``Place it into plate''), so the prompt --- not task progress --- determines the target. The evaluated subtask model is trained on 846 such episodes.

The teleoperated motions are markedly slower than the baseline: precise fast control is hard with the gamepad, so the operator moved deliberately, giving trajectories that differ from the LightwheelAI Baseline in velocity profile, not only in subtask-level segmentation.

A key design choice is \textbf{tailing}: every episode is extended with 20 terminal freeze frames (one action chunk) that repeat the final observation and state, so the command turns into a hold once the subtask completes. This teaches the policy to \textbf{stay idle at a subtask boundary} instead of flowing into the next phase, which serves three roles. During manual testing, one can type a prompt, watch the policy execute it, then type the next one while the arm holds its pose and keeps its grasp --- no need to pause inference between prompts. At run time, the same property makes the policy tolerate an orchestrator that does not advance instantly --- e.g.\ a higher-latency external vision-based controller --- holding rather than drifting while it waits. And the arm coming to rest is itself a natural completion signal that a higher-level controller can detect. The resulting SmolVLA model is a subtask executor: on reaching a terminal configuration it settles and waits.

\subsubsection{Autonomous Data Generation}
\label{subsec:autonomous}

Teleoperated demonstrations are collected by hand and do not scale --- and grasping, the step that most needs data, is the hardest to collect. The alternative is to let the system improve itself: run the policy, keep what works, and retrain on it, reusing the orchestrated pipeline unchanged.

\textbf{The loop.} Each autonomous rollout mixes successful and failed subtasks; the successful trajectories, taken from the policy's own behaviour, are kept and the rest discarded, giving the \textbf{Auto} set. Target redirection makes this productive: after a failed grasp the orchestrator moves to another orange instead of wasting the rollout, so even a hard scene yields usable subtasks.

\textbf{A basic form of reinforcement learning.} Keeping the successes and retraining is reinforcement learning in a simple form --- reward-weighted behavioural cloning (\cref{sec:related}) with a single-bit reward: one if the subtask succeeded, zero otherwise. That bit credits the whole trajectory at once, with no way to weigh individual action chunks, and without directed exploration the loop will not discover radically new strategies. It is not mere replay, though: each episode randomises the orange positions and camera pose, and the flow-matching policy samples actions stochastically, so the harvested successes cover states and motions absent from the demonstrations.

\textbf{Turning misgrabs into usable data.} A grasp counts as a success even when the policy closes on a different orange than requested. Rather than discard the episode, the pipeline automatically rewrites its instruction to name the orange \emph{actually} grasped --- identified from privileged state, with no human in the loop --- a hindsight relabelling of the goal~\cite{andrychowicz2017her}. Prompt and grasp now agree, so the episode becomes a valid demonstration of the correct instruction-to-target mapping: a mis-aimed attempt becomes extra, correctly-labelled aiming supervision instead of being thrown away.

Retained episodes are balanced by scene state so early scenes are not over-represented; the 414 Auto episodes merge with the 846 Teleop episodes into \textbf{Teleop+Auto} (1260 episodes). Whether the extra data helps is tested in \cref{sec:experiments}.

The data is organised as subtask episodes, roughly balanced across the three subtasks, so merging Teleop and Auto raises volume without skewing the mix. \Cref{fig:dataset_composition} shows the per-subtask distribution of episode lengths by source (frames at 30\,fps, including the 20-frame terminal hold).

Two features are relevant to later results. GRASP dominates the length budget --- two to three times longer than LIFT or PLACE, with by far the heaviest tail --- because it includes the variable search-and-approach phase before contact. The Auto episodes are shorter and less dispersed than their Teleop counterparts, which likely reflects how the episodes end rather than the motions themselves: Teleop fragments were cut by hand, when the operator judged the subtask done, so they often linger past completion; Auto fragments are cut automatically the moment the success check fires (an orange lifted past its height threshold, a grasp meeting its force and geometry checks, a place with the oranges at rest and the gripper clear).

\subsubsection{Matched Monotask Comparison}
\label{subsec:monotask}

A subtask model differs from the LightwheelAI Baseline in both training distribution and formulation, so that comparison cannot isolate subtasking. Each subtask model is therefore paired with a \textbf{monotask} counterpart trained on the \emph{same} trajectories: per-subtask instructions replaced by the single fixed prompt ``Place the orange into plate'', freeze tails removed, run end-to-end without the orchestrator's checkpoints. The pair sees identical demonstrations and differs only in framing, at both training and execution time; every run uses the same 5000-step budget.

The match is in the data, not in the supervision --- intentionally, since the supervision is part of what defines each formulation: the freeze tails teach the subtask model to settle at each boundary, while the monotask model never sees boundaries and keeps acting under one prompt. The point of the pairing is that both models learn from the same trajectories, so any performance gap reflects the formulation --- monotask versus orchestrated subtask, with everything each entails --- rather than a difference in training data.
