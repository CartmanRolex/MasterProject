\begin{abstract}
Long-horizon robotic manipulation fails not only when a single step fails, but when execution cannot recover afterwards: a learned policy may stagnate on an unreachable target or drift into states its training never covered. This project asks whether wrapping a Vision-Language-Action (VLA) policy in an execution-level recovery structure makes a long-horizon pick-and-place task more robust. In a simulated Isaac Sim task where an SO-101 arm must place three oranges into a plate, the task is decomposed into language-conditioned subtasks --- grasp, lift, place --- sequenced by a finite-state orchestrator that verifies each subtask's physical outcome and recovers at two levels: retrying the same orange, or redirecting to a different one to preserve task progress, enabled by a scripted spatial reset. A SmolVLA policy is fine-tuned as the subtask executor on a custom teleoperated dataset with randomised pick order, and the orchestrated system is reused to generate further training data autonomously. On a 100-episode seeded benchmark, decomposition makes the system rarely fail completely --- it almost always places at least one orange --- yet does not raise full-task completion: the binding constraint is grasp acquisition, which lies upstream of every checkpoint and is left untouched by both the recovery structure and the autonomous data. The work therefore localises the bottleneck to the subtask policy rather than the orchestration around it.

\end{abstract}
